---
76                    Sri Rudra Bhashyam
---

by invoking, through a ruse, the teachings of the Gautama school, [the text says]: "All of them, having reached Deva-Isha (the Lord of gods), Shankara (Shiva), and the imperishable Vishnu, praised them with various hymns, like servants who partake of the remnants of a feast" — thus presenting their supplication in the presence of Shankara and Vishnu. [It is then stated:] "Therefore, for the protection of those outside the Veda and for the deluding of sinners, we shall create [deluding] scriptures, O Vrishabhavaja (bull-bannered one)! Thus urged by Madhava (Vishnu), the destroyer of Mura (Murari), Hara (Shiva) composed deluding scriptures; and Keshava (Vishnu) too, urged by Shiva, [did likewise]. The Kapala (skull-doctrine), the Laguda, the Shakta (Shakti-based), the Bhairava, the Eastern and Western [doctrines], the Pancharatra (five-night doctrine), the Pashupata (doctrine of the Lord of beasts), and thousands of others" — so it is stated: that for the protection of the most sinful ones outside the Veda, [the Lord] set in motion the Tantric paths such as the Pashupata, the Pancharatra, and others. The meaning of "for the purpose of delusion (vimohana)" is this: in the Tantras such as the Pashupata and Pancharatra, there is indeed teaching of content contrary to the Veda — for instance, the doctrine of the Lord as merely an instrumental cause (kevala-nimitta-ishvara-vada), descriptions of the origination of individual souls (jiva-utpatti), and censure of the Veda in relation to these matters. All of that is without epistemic authority (apramana); indeed, the lack of authority of those portions has been established in the Brahma-Mimamsa (Vedanta philosophy). The purpose of the censure of the Veda and the description of things contrary to it in those texts is: to delude those who, though they are followers of the Veda, due to the force of sin develop disbelief in the gods. That delusion of theirs is for their ultimate benefit. For having heard the censure of the Veda placed in those Tantric texts, they hold [the Tantras] in high regard and, abandoning the Veda, enter the Tantric path, worship the Supreme Lord by those prescribed methods, and — with their accumulated sin of hatred of the Veda cleansed by that merit — in a subsequent birth they take up Vedic discipline and become fulfilled.
