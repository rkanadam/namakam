[Mantra 2:]
Obeisance to the great one (Kakubha), to the one bearing a sword (nishangin), to the Lord of secret thieves (stena) — obeisance.

Now the second [commentary]: — "Namah kakubhaya" (Obeisance to the great one). Obeisance to the great (mahate) Kakubha (the lofty-peaked one), to the sword-bearer (nishangin), to the Lord of secret thieves (stena — those who steal in stealth). Here, in the expectation of an opposite to the greatness (mahattva) expressed by the word "kakubha," it is the thieves (stena) mentioned in the same sentence who serve as that opposite; and thus the connection is that greatness serves as the reason for lordship over thieves. Accordingly, the word "kakubhaya" expresses greatness as the reason for His lordship over thieves. The word "nishangin" (sword-bearer) displays the possession of equipment appropriate to the thieving activity. Thus ends the second.

[Mantra 3:]
Obeisance to the sword-and-quiver-bearer, to the arrow-holder, to the Lord of open thieves (taskara) — obeisance.

Now the third [commentary]: — "Namo nishangin" (Obeisance to the sword-bearer). "Nishanga" means an arrow held in the hand at a bow; "ishu-dhi" means a quiver bound to the back; obeisance to the one possessing both of these; obeisance to the Lord of open thieves (taskara — those who steal openly, in daylight). Here the qualifying epithets are to be understood as showing the form appropriate to the lordship over open thieves. Thus ends the third.
