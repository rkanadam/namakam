Such is the purport of that verse. The same is stated in the Mahiṣrāṣṭa text as well — beginning with "mahokṣaḥ khaṭvāṅgam" ("the great bull, the skull-staff") and concluding with "na hi svātmārāmaṃ viṣaya-mṛga-tṛṣṇā bhramayati" ("for indeed the mirage of sense-objects does not delude one who delights in the Self alone"). Thus, the wearing of the hide (kṛtti) as garment signifies the cessation of all interest in sense objects for one who revels in the Self alone, and is therefore a mark (lakṣaṇa) of his gracious intent (prīti).

With respect to "bearing the Pināka" — here the derivation (pravṛtti-nimitta, the etymological basis) of the word pināka is intended, which comes from the root pā (to protect); and by the grammatical rule "pināka and others" (pinākaādyaś ca), the shortening and augmentation of the vowel are established. The bearing of the Pināka — which is preeminently an instrument of protection — is a sign of his gracious disposition. The seer of this mantra is Vairāja. The meter is Triṣṭubh. The deity is Śaṃsu. Meditation verse: "One should meditate upon the god who is the abode of all auspiciousness (maṅgalāyatana), who is ever youthful and supremely beautiful, who comes approaching in the form of a forest-wanderer, bearing the Pināka bow." This mantra is primarily for averting dangers from royal power, robbers, enemies, sudden attacks, threats to life, and similar adversities.

    vikiriṃd vilohita namaste astu bhagavaḥ |
    yās te sahasraṃ hetayo 'nyam asminn nivaapantu tāḥ ||

    O Vikiriṃd (Scatterer of sins) — O Vilohita (Red one), salutation to you, O Blessed One (bhagavaḥ).
    May your thousand weapons (hetayaḥ), all of them,
    fall upon another, not upon us.
