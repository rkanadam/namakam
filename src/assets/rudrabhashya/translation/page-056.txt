56                    SRI RUDRA BHASHYAM

Even though the Lord champions [His devotees'] cause, one might ask: how
can the suffering resulting from the workings of Yama and other independent
deities not occur? To dispel this doubt, the word prathama (foremost) is
used. [The Lord is described as] "yo devanam prathamah purastadavikarako
rudro maharshih" (the foremost of the gods, pre-eminent, Rudra the great seer);
"tameshvaranam prathamam maheshvaram tam daivatam prathamam ca daivatam"
(the first among lords, the great lord, the first among divinities and the
foremost divinity); "yo devah sarvadeveshu mahadeva iti smritah, tasmai
namastuh kurvana divi tishthanti devatah" (the God who is known among all
gods as Mahadvea — the gods who salute Him dwell in heaven) — such [texts]
from the Shruti, Smriti, Itihasa (epics), and Purana establish that the Lord is
supreme among all gods. Since He who champions [His devotees'] cause is
foremost [among all gods], there is no scope for the fear of suffering inflicted
by subordinate gods.

Not only [freedom from] suffering caused by other gods, but also the supreme
status of being worshipped by all gods [is obtained by the devotee] — because
the Lord, by virtue of being the indwelling controller (antaryami) of all those
[gods], the singular deity who champions [the devotee] became the one whose
favor is sought — this is indicated by [the word] "daivyah," which alludes to
His quality of being the indwelling controller of all gods.

By the word "bhishak" (physician), using the analogy of the physician as
described in the Shiva Purana — "just as a physician who knows the cause of
illness causes harm to the patient through treatment like surgery, cauterization,
and so on, yet in doing so heals him — there is no cruelty here, only
compassion" — the supreme compassion of the Lord is set forth. And through
this, even while striking the ahis (serpents and evil forces) and yatudhanyahs,
His compassion toward even those [beings] is shown. Just as a wound-physician
(vrana-vaidya, a surgeon), though momentarily causing pain through surgery,
cauterization, and the like, ultimately brings happiness to the patient by
healing his wound and is thus compassionate — likewise, the Lord who is the
physician of worldly existence (samsara-vaidya), even while striking the ahis
and yatudhanyahs, by removing the sins that are the root cause of their
future [suffering] and making them pure, progressively leads them to happiness
— therefore His compassion is not diminished. This [meaning] is shown here.

Since He [acts] while declaring [His protection] — not [merely] praying "may
[the Lord] destroy" — [and since] the phrase "adhy abocat" (He has declared)
rather than "jambhayatu" (may He destroy) shows the Lord's faultless
sovereignty — and since [in this mantra] the destruction of the ahis, yatudhanyahs,
and the deities that impel them (such as Yama) is attributed to the Lord Himself
acting independently — and since the Lord, having first protected the devotee
by destroying the ahis and yatudhanyahs, when [other] gods come and ask
"how is this sinner to be protected?" makes the partisan declaration "This one
is mine — how does he have sins?" — this is the sequence. Hence even for
great sinners — having destroyed the messengers of Yama who were about to
take them, like hunters and others, through his own emissaries, when Yama,
Chitragupta, and others report the incident to the Lord and He has them
brought to Kailasa — [the Lord says:] "Even if inadvertently, these performed
acts such as throwing bilva leaves [at Shiva's image] and prostrations; therefore
they belong to Me, and you have no authority over them." Such stories are
repeated frequently in the Puranas. With this understanding, the fact that in
this [mantra] "raksha ca no adhi ca deva bruhi" (protect us, and also champion
our cause, O God) — protection comes first and the declaration of partisanship
second — is to be comprehended. This is why this mantra is the remover of all
afflictions from serpents, Vishnu [adversaries], rakshasas, pishachas, and
all [such beings]. Hence its designation as a kavacha (armor/protective mantra)
— as stated by the revered Bodhayana: "adhy abocat iti kavacham" (the mantra
'adhy abocat' is the armor).
