Sri Rudra Bhashyam                    133

---

"Drape andhasaspata" and "O Nilalohita" — these two forms of address are laden with implicit reasoning: since He is the one who leads creatures to wretched states through fear, disease and the like, He also has the power to remove those same afflictions; and since He is the master and protector of devotees (bhakta-svami), He can see and protect them. The epithet Nilalohita (blue-red one) implies His being accompanied by Uma (the goddess Parvati), and this suggests that His compassion is amplified by Her power — thus hinting that the granting of freedom from fear, disease and the like is implied. In this way, it is shown that the acts of punishing and nurturing that the Lord performs do not taint Him with partiality or cruelty (vaishamya or nairghrinya) — this is itself made known by the Vedic technical term "daridra" (the utterly poor one, implying that He acts without personal interest or favoritism). The Rishi of this mantra is Pulastya. The deity is Bhairava Rudra. The meter is Aastara-Pankti. The meditation verse is: "Rudra-Bhairava, adorned in the fearsome form — wearing anklets, a half-neck garment and a small drum; his body smeared with ash, bearing a trident, with coiled hair, an auspicious mark on the forehead, adorned with ear-rings, with jingling ornaments; with brilliant hair-ornaments — such is the Rudra-Deva in the Bhairava form, worshipped by all." This mantra yields the fruit of complete pacification of fear from all quarters, overcoming disease, and the direct experience of Bhairava. Mastery of the mantra is achieved through ten thousand repetitions (japa), preceded by a fast of one day.

           Ya te rudra shiva tanu shivavisvahe-
           bheshaji shiva rudrasya bheshaji taya no
           mriha jivase

           (That auspicious form of yours, O Rudra, O all-healing one,
           that is the medicine of Rudra — with that, make us glad that
           we may live)
