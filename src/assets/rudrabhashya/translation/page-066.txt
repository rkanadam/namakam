64                    Śrī Rudra Bhāṣyam

---

    "Break off the tips of the arrows and be Śiva — become gracious and well-disposed towards us."

Having thus prayed for the scattering [of the arrows], not finding peace of mind even with that much, [the devotee] now prays for the blunting of the arrows: "Avatatya" — the words "śata" (hundred) and "sahasra" (thousand) are used to denote infinity; O Sahasrākṣa (thousand-eyed = of infinite sight), O Śateṣughe (hundred-quivered = of infinite arrows) — the infinitude of arrows is itself implied by the infinitude of the quivers. For in ordinary life, an archer of limited sight and a limited quiver can somehow be dealt with by avoiding his line of sight or by exhausting his arrows; but you who have unlimited vision and unlimited arrows cannot be evaded by any means — such is the hidden meaning of the double form of address. Therefore, in this matter which is difficult for me to get around by my own efforts, you yourself, being gracious, having "avatatya" — having put the string back on the bow while the arrow is still notched — having "niśīrya" — broken off and blunted the tips of the arrows (śalyānam mukhā — the points of the shafts) — even after this is done, while the cruel archer still remains, the mind does not rest. So he further specifies: "Śiva iti" — meaning "be of a gentle countenance" (saumyākāra). For gentle outward appearance alone, without the corresponding inner kindness, is not trustworthy. Therefore "be sumanāḥ (well-disposed in heart)." Meaning: by seeing that this one has surrendered (śaraṇāgata), may you become tender with the nectar of compassion. Thus [ends] the eleventh [mantra].
