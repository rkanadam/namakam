                        Upoddhāta (Introduction)                             19

[Continuing the discussion of Śrī Rudra's status as the supreme Upaniṣad and the eligibility of all āśramas for its recitation]

...destroys [ignorance] — brahma-vidyā (knowledge of Brahman) directly, while in the Vedānta texts it functions as an instrument [of that knowledge], just as in the phrase "the Chāndogya is the living one" and the like. This twofold derivation is highly natural in the case of Śrī Rudra.

Therefore, the word 'atyāśramī' (supreme renunciant) in the passage from the Vājasaneya tradition beginning "students go..." is to be understood as a synecdoche (upalakṣaṇa) that stands for all four āśramas (stages of life); the meaning is: anyone belonging to any of the four āśramas who recites Śrī Rudra daily — always or even once — attains liberating knowledge.

Thus, just as the opening phrase "Whoever recites the Śatarudrīya" and the concluding phrase "Let the supreme renunciant recite it" form a unified sentence (ekavākyatā), with the word 'atyāśramī' denoting all persons eligible to recite Śrī Rudra. The statements of the Sūta Saṃhitā — "By householders, forest-dwellers, and celibate students alike" — are similarly to be interpreted as supporting this unity of meaning.

Since it is thus established: The general subject indicated in the opening phrase "Whoever recites the Śatarudrīya..." satisfies the expectation for an agent (kartṛ-kāṅkṣā) required by the concluding act of recitation (japa) in "Let him always, or even once, recite it" — one may then ask: What further purpose is served by inserting the word 'atyāśramī' to indicate the general agent by way of synecdoche? Hear this explanation: In the opening of the Kaivalya Upaniṣad, the teaching is given — "Not by ritual action, not by progeny, not by wealth; only by renunciation do some attain immortality" — thus teaching renunciation as the means to liberation.
