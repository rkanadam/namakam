---
4                              Sri Rudra Bhashyam.
---

[Continuing discussion of the Rudradhyaya's glory:]

...the greatness of the Rudra chapter. Let us now trace it.

Although the statement "Yajnavalkya replied: 'By the Shataraudriya'" establishes only that the Shataraudriya is the means of liberation through recitation (japa), nonetheless, upon careful examination of the structure of the question and the answer, it is established that the Shataraudriya is supreme among all Upanishads. We explain this as follows:

The true state of affairs is this: the Upanishads are means of liberation — liberation being attained exclusively through the knowledge (jnana) of the unity of Brahman and Atman — and since the Upanishads serve as means of liberation both by positive co-presence (anvaya) and negative co-absence (vyatireka), they are the Upanishads meant for recitation as a means of liberation. If this state of affairs were already known to the brahmacharins, then the question would not arise [coherently]; for once it is known that Upanishads in general are means of liberation through recitation, it makes no sense to ask again, "By the recitation of what does one attain immortality?" And if they did not yet know this, then the answer "By the Shataraudriya" would also not be appropriate. For when the general truth — that Upanishads are means of liberation through japa — is not yet known, it would be inappropriate to answer simply "By the Shataraudriya"; rather, the fitting reply would be "By an Upanishad." Otherwise, by the logical principle "a specific selection serves to exclude what is left out" (visheshagrahana sheshapratisheddhartha), there would arise the unwanted conclusion that Upanishads other than the Shataraudriya are not means of liberation through recitation — just as if, in response to "Who in this town is learned?", one says "Devadatta is learned," it implies that others like Yajnadatta are not learned. Therefore, what must be understood is that the question and answer share the same reference point: having established in general that all Upanishads are means of liberation through recitation, the question asks which among them is the highest means of recitation; the answer being: the Shataraudriya — "By the Shataraudriya." Therefore, when the doubt arises "How then is the Shataraudriya supreme among all Upanishads?", the answer given is: "These are verily the names of the Immortal" and so forth. The meaning of "etani" (these) is: the names — occurring in the Shataraudriya — are names of the Immortal (amrita), i.e., of the eternally liberated Supreme Lord. "What then follows from this?" — The text continues: "By these verily one becomes immortal" — the particle "ha va" here being emphatic — "by these very names alone one becomes free." Here, the idea is set aside that the contemplative knowledge of the Brahman-Atman unity established in the Upanishad [other than japa] is needed. The sacred names of the Supreme Lord contained in this text alone, by mere recitation (japa), bestow liberation; this is not the case with other Upanishads — hence its supremacy over all Upanishads. The particle "etani ha va" also hints at the tradition of its occurrence across one hundred branches [of the Veda]. This further demonstrates that although all the names are indeed names of the Supreme Lord — as the Shruti declares in "Ima Rudraya sthiradhanvane girah" (these praises are for Rudra of the firm bow), showing that all words in the universe directly denote the Supreme Lord; and as Ashvalayana, after introducing Shiva, states "All these are verily his names" — nevertheless, because of its being well known by its presence across one hundred branches [of the Veda] and as the foremost means of liberation, it is pre-eminently called "the names," just as among the thirty-six days that constitute a certain sacrificial rite, twelve are designated as the principal days by the term "dvadashaha" (twelve-day sacrifice), even though all are days of equal standing.
