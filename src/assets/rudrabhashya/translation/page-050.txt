— sharavye brahma-samshita' (the brahman-sharpened quiver)." Or alternatively,
the word sharavya is derived from the root "shru" with the sense of striking:
sharp arrows (sharuh) are weapons; their collection (sharavar sharavah) is the
quiver; may that quiver be auspicious (shiva). With that sharavya — this is
a synecdoche (upalakshana) — [meaning] with that bow and with that arrow —
mrida nah (grant happiness to us, make us happy). The seer of this mantra is
of the lineage of Atri (Atreya); the meter is Anushtup; the deity is Shambhu.

The dhyana-shloka (verse for meditation) is: "May one meditate upon the
god [Rudra] smiling, standing in a chariot, blazing with radiance together
with the Goddess, with arms adorned with bows and quivers, of heroic form,
praised by crowds of gods."

Perfection (purashcarana-siddhi) is attained by continuous recitation for
twelve days, [performed] at midnight in a way that does not conflict with
one's daily obligatory duties (nitya-karma). This mantra is especially
efficacious for the removal of famine, civil unrest, and similar calamities.

Thus [ends] the second mantra.

        That form of Yours, O Rudra, which is auspicious, which does not
        terrify, which illumines freedom from sin — with that peaceful form,
        O Girisha (Lord of the Mountain), shine forth upon us on all sides.

Having thus prayed in the second [mantra] for worldly happiness, and having
prayed in the third for the knowledge that is the cause of liberation (moksha)
— "ya te rudra" — O Rudra, that form (tanuh) of Yours which is well-known,
as is heard in the Shruti: "tasya ete tanuvau ghore 'nya shiva 'nya" (Of Him
these two forms exist — one terrible, the other auspicious); and in the
Mahabharata as well: "The two forms of that God are known to brahmin
seers of the divine: one terrible and one auspicious, and those again a
hundredfold" —
