This doubt is resolved as follows: the Śruti does not teach identity with the body designated by the word "sva" (the individual self at the bodily level); rather, it teaches the identity of pure consciousness (śuddha caitanya) — the inner referent, stripped of limiting adjuncts (upādhi) — running through the high and low forms of embodied existence as their very essence.

This point has been clarified explicitly by the Lord Himself in the Gītā: "The learned see with equal vision the brāhmaṇa endowed with learning and humility, the cow, the elephant, the dog, and the outcaste dog-eater alike." Nor does this imply even the slightest connection of the Supreme Lord with the faults and qualities of those limiting adjuncts; just as the sun, when reflected in a pond, in wine, or in urine, contracts no connection with the qualities or defects of those media — this is universally established and without fault.

Thus concludes this fourth anuvāka (chapter), which consists of seventeen Yajus mantras (yajurātmaka) forming a group of inner mantras, all in the form of obeisances offered alternately. It constitutes a single mantra-composition. Its ṛṣi (seer) is Rudra himself; the chandas (metre) is Mahāvirāṭ; the devatā (presiding deity) is Śambhu. The dhyāna (meditation verse) is: "One should meditate upon Hara — all his limbs smeared with sacred ash, adorned with a crown of matted locks, riding the bull, and attended by the Lord of the Gaṇas." This mantra removes diseases such as rājayakṣmā (tuberculosis), fever, and threefold bodily disorders (sannipāta), and other ailments. Perfection (siddhi) of the mantra is attained by reciting it one thousand times preceded by a Prājāpatya vow (sārdhha-prājāpatya).

                    Thus ends the Fourth Anuvāka.
