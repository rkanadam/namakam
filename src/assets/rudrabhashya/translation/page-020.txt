18                        Śrī Rudra Bhāṣyam

Furthermore, the instruction for the recitation of Śrī Rudra (Śrī Rudra japa) is confirmed by thousands of Purāṇic statements that accord with the Devadūna tradition — such as: "The mantra known as Śatarudrīya (the Hundred-Names-of-Rudra) is to be recited by householders, forest-dwellers, and celibate students alike." These Purāṇic testimonies contradict any restriction on who may recite it. Nor should it be said that the injunction "Let the supreme renunciant (atyāśramī) recite it" applies exclusively to sannyāsins (renunciants) in the context of the Rudra being prescribed for liberation; for Śrī Rudra is available to sannyāsins precisely by virtue of its status as an Upaniṣad (esoteric scripture) — "He gave the Rudra chapter, which is the essence of all the Upaniṣads" — and because the injunction "Let him always recite the Upaniṣad" mandates the recitation of Upaniṣads for sannyāsins.

Nor is there any lack of evidence for Śrī Rudra's status as an Upaniṣad. The Śiva Purāṇa declares: "The Rudra chapter has been established as the essence of all Upaniṣads. Whether one seeks enjoyment, liberation, or expiation — there is no path other than the Rudra Upaniṣad." Similarly, learned authorities such as Bhaṭṭa Bhāskara have used the term 'Upaniṣad' in connection with it, based on the recognized sense of the word upaniṣad. Indeed, the word 'upaniṣad' is used in two derived senses within the tradition: (1) Those who dwell near (upa-niṣad) the Supreme Brahman — meaning the Vedānta texts — since they aim directly at Brahman, unlike the ritual sections (karma-kāṇḍa) which prescribe worshipful sacrifices as indirect means; or (2) The word 'upaniṣad' is derived from the verbal root ṣad preceded by the prefixes upa and ni, with the sense of 'that which destroys' — meaning it destroys avidyā (ignorance), the root cause of saṃsāra (transmigration), and thus designates brahma-vidyā (knowledge of Brahman) directly. As an instrument of that knowledge, it also applies, as in "the Chāndogya is the living one" and similar usages. This twofold derivation is especially apt in the case of Śrī Rudra. Therefore, the mention of 'atyāśramī' (supreme renunciant) in the passage beginning "those who go, being students" in the Vājasaneya tradition is a synecdoche (upalakṣaṇa) for all āśramas (stages of life) in general; the meaning is that anyone in any of the four āśramas who recites Śrī Rudra daily — always, or even once — attains liberating knowledge.

In this way, the opening phrase "Whoever studies the Śatarudrīya" and the closing phrase "Let the supreme renunciant recite it" form a unified sentence (ekavākyatā), with the word 'atyāśramī' serving as a synecdoche for any person eligible to recite Śrī Rudra. The Sūta Saṃhitā likewise confirms this unity of meaning: "Whoever a man may be that recites the Śatarudrīya with faith, day after day — such a one, even after death in the great Viṣṇu's domain, attains lordly knowledge through that knowledge, and by that higher knowledge reaches the supreme destination. By householders, forest-dwellers, and celibate students alike..."
