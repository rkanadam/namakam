Accordingly, in a subsequent birth they take up Vedic discipline and become fulfilled. It is precisely for this reason that, in the Vasistha-Lainga [Purana] (a scripture associated with the sage Vasishtha and the Linga tradition), the following is heard regarding the case of Shandilya — one who, though a follower of the Veda, abandoned his Vedic discipline and worshipped Vishnu through the initiatory rites of the Pancharatra and similar paths — wherein a reproach is directed at him, followed by a boon that he will return to the Vedic path: "O Shandilya, you worshipped Me by resorting to another means. With that worship I am not pleased; your affection [for Me] is dear to Me. O Shandilya, even by that path you have performed worship of Me. Therefore, in time you will proceed along the Vedic path." The forbearance of the Supreme Lord toward sinners — even though it appears like disdain toward them — ultimately results in their well-being, and thus does not constitute a fault. Indeed, for this reason, in the avatars (divine descents) such as that of the Buddha, the deluding function of the Lord Narayana is repeatedly proclaimed in many texts. That is not a defect in Him. Thus, all of the foregoing here is meant by the phrase "for the purpose of delusion, scriptures [were created]."

In this manner, there are also many other scriptural passages on this subject; all of them have been cited by us in our work Pashanda-Gaja-Kesarini (Lion-against-the-Elephant-of-Heresy); they are to be consulted there. Thus it has been stated that, for the protection of those outside the Veda, the all-compassionate Lord set in motion the Tantric paths — and this is indicated by the phrase "tri-shimate" (pertaining to the three-peaked [mountain], or "of the three boundaries"). Beyond that, [an objection arises regarding] the inequality (vaishamya) and cruelty (nairghrinya) of the Supreme Lord who creates some as deservers of Vedic authority and others as being outside the Veda. This objection is addressed by the word "saspinjaraya" (tinged with yellow-green), which denotes a particular mixed color and, by extension, the predominance of the quality of the three gunas (sattva, rajas, tamas — the three fundamental qualities of nature).
