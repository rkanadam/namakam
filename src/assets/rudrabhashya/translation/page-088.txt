---
                    Third Anuvaka
---

[Mantra 1:]
Obeisance to the one who overpowers, to the one who frightens deeply, to the Lord of arrow-releasing [female forces] — obeisance.

Now in the third anuvaka, as in the second, the Lord is saluted through the first eight mantras as the Lord of all (sarvesha). Therein the first [is]: — "Namah sahamanaya" (Obeisance to the overpowering one). Obeisance to the one who overpowers (sahamana) enemies, that is, to the one who is intolerant of wrongs done to devotees — such is the meaning. "Nichyadhi" means one who is exceedingly frightened [of sin] — obeisance to Him; "avyadhinyah" means heroic armies who are restrained from fleeing [by fear of the Lord] — obeisance to their Lord. Here, the two qualifying epithets "sahamanaya" (overpowering) and "nichyadhine" (deeply frightened) contain within them the reason for His lordship over the avyadhinis (heroic armies): since He who is both supremely frightened [of sin] among the frightened ones, and capable of overpowering all opposition, is most eminent among them, it is fitting that He be their lord. If the word "sahamana" is taken in the sense of being forbearing toward the wrongs of devotees, then there is this special aptness to be noted: for if the Supreme Lord were to destroy [beings] excessively on account of their own [sins], striking more severely than all others, then the avyadhinis (armies), the world-protectors, would all become like vessels emptied of water — for since no being is free from sin, all living beings would be subject to destruction by the Supreme Lord on account of their offenses. [Therefore,] addressing the objection that such unavoidable destruction [of all] would follow, [the text] says "sahamanaya" — indicating that since the Lord is naturally forbearing toward the wrongs of devotees, this objection does not apply. Thus ends the first.
