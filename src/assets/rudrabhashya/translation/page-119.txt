"Thus says the Śruti" — in accord with all these supporting testimonies and consistent with those corroborations. Not only does the Lord's association with Umā account for His role as the bestower of liberation alone — but indeed all His greatness: omniscience, omnipotence (sarveśvaratva), His inner presence in all (sarvāntaryāmitva), His being the cause of everything (sarvaikāraṇatva), and so forth — all this glory is but the play of the supreme power (para-śakti-vilāsa). Thus the Śrutis proclaim: "His supreme power is heard of as manifold and various — natural, consisting of knowledge, strength, and action" — and so forth. The Itihāsas and Purāṇas elaborate upon this, and the learned affirm: "Power is the body of this deity; it is the inner Self, knowledge, action, and the instrument — the cluster of desires, the abode, the coverings — all this is You, O crowned one with tender shoots [Bālaśaṭhāṅkumau, epithet for Śiva]. What is there that you are not, O dwelling place of all?" And: "If the deity were not thus [united with Śakti], truly He would not be capable of even a quiver." Therefore, since the immense glory of such supreme power of Umā is precisely the reason why the Taittirīya Upaniṣad, having used the term "abhīkāpataye" (to the lord of the beloved one), further employs the term "amāpataye" (to the husband of Amā, i.e., Umā) — whereby the Lord is saluted in His capacity as the Lord of Umā — this much for now.

      Homage to Tāmra and to Aruṇa.

      Now the second mantra — in which the Lord is shown to be the sole object of worship for all, being directly perceptible in the form of Āditya (the Sun), the guide and lord of all, and the bestower of all auspiciousness: "Namo tāmrāya" — At the time of sunrise, [the solar orb appears] copper-coloured (tāmra) in its interior; thereafter, slightly reddish (aruṇa); and from the conjunctive particle "ca," it takes on many more colours above.
