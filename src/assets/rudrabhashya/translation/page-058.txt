56                    SRI RUDRA BHASHYAM

Even though the Lord champions [His devotees'] cause, one might ask:
how can the suffering resulting from the workings of Yama and other
independent deities not occur [for the devotee]? To dispel this doubt,
the word prathama (foremost, primary) is used. [Scriptural texts confirm:]
"yo devanam prathamah purastadavikarako rudro maharshih" (Rudra, the
great seer, who is the foremost and pre-eminent among gods); "tameshvaranam
prathamam maheshvaram tam daivatam prathamam ca daivatam" (the first
among lords, the great lord, the first among divinities and the foremost
divinity); "yo devah sarvadeveshu mahadeva iti smritah, tasmai namastuh
kurvana divi tishthanti devatah" (the God who is known among all gods
as Mahadeva — the gods who offer salutation to Him dwell in heaven).
Such texts from the Shruti, Smriti, Itihasa, and Purana establish that the
Lord is the supreme among all gods. Since the Lord who champions [His
devotees'] cause is the foremost [of all gods], there is no room for fear
of suffering inflicted by subordinate gods.

Not only [freedom from] suffering caused by other gods, but also the
supreme status of being worshipped by all the gods [is attained] — because
by virtue of the Lord being the indwelling controller (antaryami) of all
those [gods], the one Lord who has become a partisan advocate [for the
devotee] is the sole supreme deity — this is shown by the word "daivyah,"
which implies the quality of being the indwelling controller of all the gods.

Through the word "bhishak" (physician), using the analogy of the physician
as set forth in the Shiva Purana: "A physician who knows the diagnosis
causes harm to the patient through surgery, cauterization, and the like;
yet here there is no cruelty — compassion alone motivates him." With this
analogy, the supreme compassion of the Lord is expressed; and by this,
His compassion even toward the ahis (serpents) and yatudhanyahs whom He
destroys is demonstrated. Just as a surgeon (vrana-vaidya), though causing
the wounded patient momentary pain through surgery and cauterization, yet
heals his wounds and thereby brings him happiness — and is thus
compassionate — so too the Lord who is the physician of worldly existence
(samsara-vaidya), even while striking the ahis and yatudhanyahs, purifies
them by removing the sins that are the root cause of their further births,
and thereby progressively leads them to happiness — therefore His
compassion is not diminished.

The fact that [the text says] "adhy abocat" (He has declared, He has spoken
on behalf) rather than "jambhayatu" (may He destroy) shows the Lord's
faultless sovereignty. Not that He, being incapable of subduing the ahis
and yatudhanyahs Himself, propitiates them with a request: "spare this one
who is mine" — on the contrary, [He is perfectly capable:] He Himself first
protects the devotee by destroying the ahis and yatudhanyahs, and when
the [other] gods come asking "how is this sinner to be protected?", He
makes the declaration "This one is mine — what sin does he have?" Hence
the procedure [shown in this mantra applies] even to great sinners.

Stories are recounted in the Puranas: hunters and others, [though sinners,]
were saved by the Lord's divine emissaries who killed the messengers of
Yama who had come to take them; brought to Kailasa, when Yama,
Chitragupta, and others reported [the sinners'] deeds to the Lord, He
declared to them: "Even if inadvertently (yad ricchaya), these performed
acts such as throwing bilva leaves [at My image] and prostrations — therefore
they are Mine; you have no authority over them." Such stories are repeatedly
narrated in the Puranas. Understanding this, the sequence in this [mantra]
where "raksha ca no adhi ca deva bruhi" (protect us, and then champion
our cause, O God) places protection first and the partisan declaration
second — this is its import. Hence this mantra is the one that removes all
afflictions from serpents, rakshasas, pishachas, and all [such beings].
This is why Bodhayana calls it a kavacha (protective armor): "adhy abocat
iti kavacham" (the verse 'adhy abocat' is the armor mantra).
