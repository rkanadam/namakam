Introduction (Upoddhatah).                                     3
---

In the Skanda Purana's Suta Samhita: "The flower of the Shataraudriya extends across one hundred branches [of the Veda]. Therefore, by the mere recitation of it, one is freed from all sins." Bhattabhaskara also states: "The Rudra Upanishad is transmitted in one hundred branches of the Yajurveda."

Alternatively, "Rudriya" means the sacred names of Rudra; "Shataraudriya" then means: "that which contains a hundred — meaning infinite — sacred names of Rudra within it." This is supported by the Jabala Upanishad's declaration: "These are verily the names of the Immortal." And the Supreme Lord himself declares in the Kurma Purana: "Hear, O sinless one, these secret names of mine."

In the Mahabharata, the Dronaparva contains a chapter called the Shataraudriya-adhyaya. There, having first proclaimed the infinite nature of the Lord's names — "In this world, this [Lord] has many names, each of which is truly meaningful" — the text cites the Veda's Shataraudriya as the authoritative support for that infinity: "And the supreme Shataraudriya found in the Veda is well known [as the source of his names]." By this, it is declared that the infinity of Rudra's names is the very basis of the designation "Shataraudriya."

Moreover, one should not object that names not listed by the Naighantuka (the Vedic lexicon, Nighantu) and similar works cannot truly be considered names of the Supreme Lord. For the Veda itself directly declares their status as names — "These are verily the names of the Immortal" — and it is not proper to abandon what is directly stated by the Veda on account of the Naighantuka. Furthermore, the Kurma Purana's statement "These are my secret names" reveals that these are secret Vedic names, and therefore any absence from the Naighantuka is naturally explained — for the Nighantu compilers concern themselves only with names used in well-known poetic works such as plays and secular poetry, and do not engage with secret Vedic passages. Indeed, well over a thousand Vedic designations can be found that are not included in the Naighantuka — for example: "Tat is verily the name of this great being," "That is the designation of water: ruja, which flows," and "That is called Ud," and so on. The absence of such names from the Naighantuka does not negate their status as names; rather, their secrecy within the Veda actually corroborates it.

[As for the objection that] their status as the Lord's names is understood from Mahabharata and Bhagavata passages such as "OM tat sat — this triad of words is declared in the Brahman" and "Like clouds blazing with lightning" — one may be surprised at such misplaced trust, whereby one who does not believe what the Veda directly states would accept something only on the authority of an Itihasa or Purana passage. Moreover, the very Mahabharata passage we have already cited — "In this world, this Lord has many names, each truly meaningful; and the supreme Shataraudriya in the Veda is the renowned source of them" — itself demonstrates this. And there are individual usages in Mahabharata and Bhagavata passages such as: "Harikeshas-tathetyu, the radiant lord with long sight," "Your form is inaccessible to Giritra, the guardians of the world, Vishnu, and the lord of the gods," "Then Midhah was proclaimed," "Then the most bountiful Midhustama was praised" — where names like Harikesha, Midhustama and others, known from the Shri Rudra, appear used [outside the Veda].
