78                    Sri Rudra Bhashyam

[word ending in] "pena" — [that the charge of] inequality and the rest has been refuted. Since He creates [beings] in accordance with their respective subtle karmic impressions (anu-karma) accumulated through beginningless ignorance, there is no occasion for the charge of inequality or cruelty. As the Shruti (Vedic scripture) declares: "One becomes meritorious through meritorious action, sinful through sinful action" — such is the import.

[Mantra 4:]
Obeisance to the one who reclines on Babhru (the bull), to the one who takes fright, to the Lord of food — obeisance.

Now the fourth [commentary]: — "Namo babhrushaaya" (Obeisance to the one who reclines on Babhru). He who bears the Supreme Lord [upon himself] is called "Babhru" — the bull; [this word is derived] by the Unadhi-sutra (a grammatical rule governing Vedic noun formations) "kubhre'tra" from the root meaning "to bear" (bibharti), with the suffix "ku" — with that suffix, with the dual [form], with the substitution [prescribed] for the verbal root, and with the prohibition of vriddhi (vowel lengthening) and guna (vowel strengthening), the form "Babhru" results. "Babhrushaaya" means "the one who reposes or dwells on Babhru" — i.e., the one borne on the bull; in the reading "Bhabhrushaya," the letter "bha" in "Babhru" is to be explained as "Bakshu." "Vinyagin" (one who specially takes fright) means one who is particularly frightened [of evil]; "ajnanaam pati" means the Lord of foodstuffs — of all kinds of edible things: things to be chewed, swallowed, licked, and drunk — and to Him obeisance. Here, by the word "babhrushaaya," lordship over the bull of dharma (righteousness) as the controller thereof is rendered fitting, since the Veda declares the sole support of dharma in the text "From sacrifice comes rain, from rain comes food" and so on. By the word "vinyagine," the non-missing of the arrow — shot by Agni in the form of an arrow — is indicated, as the Shruti says: "Those who [shoot] arrows in the eyes" and "Whose arrows are the eyes." That He whose arrows do not miss their target — the rationale is that He Himself is the most skilled archer.
