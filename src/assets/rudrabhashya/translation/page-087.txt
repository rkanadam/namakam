Now the twelfth [commentary]: — "Nama uccairghoshaya" (Obeisance to the one of loud roar). "Uccairghoshah" means one whose roar or battle-cry in wars and the like is exceedingly loud; obeisance to Him; "akrandayate" means one who causes the enemies to weep, one who destroys them — that is the import; "patthinam" means of foot-soldiers (padatin), their Lord (svamin) — obeisance — this is the meaning. Here, having established through the phrase "Lord of foot-soldiers" that the Lord's valor is relative to those who go on foot and similar [warriors], the text, in order to dispel [the implication that He depends on them], states "akrandate" (He who makes [enemies] cry) to show that He Himself has the capacity to destroy enemies. Even in destroying enemies Himself, He has no need of weapons — it is by His roar alone that [the destruction happens] — and in order to show this, the text says "uccairghoshaya" (of loud roar). Accordingly, [His] carrying of weapons and the like is merely ornamental; [this is shown by] the fact that He destroyed the cities of the demons, Manmatha (the god of love), Yama (the god of death), and others, by merely a glance and a laugh — without dependence [on weapons] — such is the import. Thus ends the twelfth.

[Mantra 13:]
Obeisance to the one who has pervaded everything, to the one who runs [to protect devotees], to the Lord of sattvic beings — obeisance.

Now the thirteenth [commentary]: — "Namah kritsnavitaya" (Obeisance to the all-pervader). "Kritsnavitah" means one by whom the entire (kritsna) world is pervaded (vita) — in the sense of being the substratum and as the inner controller (antaryamin); obeisance to Him; "dhavate" means He who runs swiftly behind [His devotees] to protect them, following after them, as the saying goes: "Like a cow [running] after her calf, the Lord of Gauri (Shiva) follows the one who runs [to Him]"; "sattvanam" means of sattvic beings (those with the quality of purity and goodness), obeisance to the Lord (svamin). Here the reason for His lordship over sattvic beings is stated by the word "dhavate" — for it is fitting that He who runs behind the sattvic ones to protect them, like a cow following her calf, should be their lord. The word "kritsnavitaya" (all-pervading) further supports that very running — since the all-pervading [Lord] is able to run after devotees from all directions. This very all-pervasiveness as the Lord's capacity to protect from everywhere is heard in the Grihya-sutra (household ritual text) of the Vajasaneyins (followers of the Yajurveda): "When one is about to enter the house, one recites [this mantra]: 'Obeisance to Rudra who sits in the house; may He bring us across auspiciously.' Going along a path, one recites: 'Obeisance to Rudra who sits on the path; may He bring us across auspiciously.' About to enter a forest, one recites: 'Obeisance to Rudra who sits in the forest; may He bring us across auspiciously.' Therefore, whatever one does anywhere, one should always say "Obeisance to Rudra" — for Rudra dwells everywhere, as the Shruti declares." This entire second anuvaka (chapter section) is one mantra. Its seer (rishi) is Manduka; its meter is Mahagayatri; its deity is Sri Rudra. [The meditation verse (dhyana) is:] "Meditate on Hara (Shiva) adorned with all limbs, bearing the moon and the Ganga, at the root of the wish-fulfilling tree (kalpavriksha), together with Uma, in deep absorption." This mantra is pre-eminent in bestowing the fruits of destruction of enemies, subjugation, and lordly power (aishvarya).

Thus ends the second anuvaka.
