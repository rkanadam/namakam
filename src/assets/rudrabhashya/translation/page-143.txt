    stuhi śrutaṃ gartasadaṃ yuvānaṃ mṛgaṇṇa bhīma-
    mupahṛtyugram | mṛḍā jaritre rudra stavāno
    'nyan te asmin niṣapantu senāḥ ||

    Praise the renowned one, who dwells in the heart-cave, ever youthful,
    who, like a lion, is fearsome and terrible to those who transgress.
    O Rudra, being praised, bring joy (mṛḍa) to one who is aged.
    May your armies fall upon another, not upon us.

Now the eighth mantra — beginning "stuhi" (praise). Here the poet addresses his own self: O my soul (ātman), praise (stuhi) the Lord. Or alternatively: O speech (vacas), praise! The reason why the Lord deserves praise is stated — "śrutam" (the renowned). He is renowned throughout all the worlds as the Supreme Lord (Parameśvara). One need not fear that such a great one would be unapproachable by my praise, because "gartasadam" — he who ever abides in the cave (garta) of the heart — "ī śvaraḥ sarvabhūtānāṃ hṛddeśe 'rjuna tiṣṭhati" (The Lord abides in the heart-region of all beings, O Arjuna) — as stated in the Smṛti (sacred scripture). Not only is he to be praised for his supremacy alone, but also for his eternally youthful, beautiful form — thus says "yuvānam" (ever youthful). Having thus established the manner of praise, the poet indicates the undesirable consequence of not praising him — "upahṛtyugram" (the terrible one who withdraws/destroys). Those who disregard the duties of varṇa (social order) and āśrama (stages of life) — the devotees who are known by their defining marks — he withdraws (upasaṃhartum) them. The simile is given — "mṛgaṇṇa bhīmam" (terrible like a lion). The particle "na" (like) indicates comparison: terrible and fearsome like a lion that has risen to tear apart an elephant. And lest one think that even such a great one as the Lord might himself be overpowered by some stronger being — thus eliminating the reason for praise — the text says "ugram" (the Terrible). He is one whom none can overpower.
