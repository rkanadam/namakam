124                    Sri Rudra Bhashyam

---

Shaiva (worldly happiness) is the joy of this embodied life; Maya (mayas) is the joy of the next world, such as heaven and the rest. Shambhu is He who manifests happiness (sham bhavati pradurbhavat); and likewise mayobhuh is He who brings forth the joys of the hereafter. And so the Shruti declares: "All other beings live sustained by mere fractions of this bliss."

           Namah shankaraya ca bhayaskaraya ca
           (Salutation to the maker of happiness and to the maker of fearfulness)

Not merely as a reservoir upon whose stored water the world depends — like a pond in which the Lord's devotees find sustenance — but also as the autonomous agent (svatantra-kartritva) who bestows worldly and transcendent happiness directly: this is shown by the tenth mantra: "Namah shankaraya" etc. Although this may seem to repeat what was said by "Namah shambhave," there is a difference: in that earlier mantra, His being the source of bliss was stated indirectly (through proximity), while here it is His direct and independent agency as bestower — that is the distinction.

           Namah shivaya ca shivataraya ca
           (Salutation to the auspicious one and to the most auspicious one)

Now, to demonstrate the worthiness of the Supreme Lord alone to be worshipped by the whole world — as the one who bestows all that is truly good, who is the crown jewel of all the Shrutis (Vedic scriptures), and who even Hari (Vishnu) and Brahma worship — this supreme mantra-king, which is the eleventh mantra and which washes away the totality of all blemishes, is now stated: "Namah shivaya." The word shiva (auspicious) is derived from the causative stem (the verb "to make auspicious"), yielding the meaning "one who bestows auspiciousness" (shivamkara). And so the Atharvashikha Shruti declares: "Shiva alone is to be meditated upon — He who makes all auspicious (shivamkara)." Here the compound shivamkara (maker of auspiciousness) is given as an explanation of the word shiva. Just as in the passage "the Lord of all lords who is endowed with all lordly powers," the compound "endowed with all lordly powers" explains the word "Lord of all" — so too here, by the same principle, the meaning of shiva is understood as shivamkara.
