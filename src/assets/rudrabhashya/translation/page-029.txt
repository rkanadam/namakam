                        Upoddhāta (Introduction)                             27

[Citing Purāṇic testimony on the supremacy of Śrī Rudra]

From the Sūta Saṃhitā: "One who recites Rudra is freed from the five great sins (mahāpātaka). He attains right knowledge (samyag-jñāna) and through it is freed from bondage. There is no recitation (japa) equal to this in truth, in śruti (Vedic revelation), or in smṛti (secondary scripture)."

Thus, having established the great glory of the Rudra chapter — as known through śruti, smṛti, Itihāsa, and Purāṇa — we now refute what has been contrived by certain modern deluded Tantrikas. They argue as follows: The Śatarudrīya is directed at Agni (the fire-deity) alone, not at the Supreme Lord (Parameśvara). For in the context of the Agnicayana (fire-altar construction ritual), [the text says]: "This [fire] is indeed Rudra — that which is Agni; thus being born, he goes everywhere, all-pervading" — identifying this Rudra with Agni as the deity being manifested at the time of the fire-layer consecration. Then: "Just as a calf, once born, seeks the udder, so does this [fire] now seek its proper share [of oblations]. Therefore, one should offer libations to it, and the sacrificer should meditate upon the Śatarudrīya — it [the fire] is thus pleased (juṣṭa)." From this injunction to offer Śatarudrīya homa (oblation) into that Agni, the conclusion follows that the Śatarudrīya is directed at Agni. They further argue: By the rule stated in Bhaṭṭa's Vārtika: "Whether by secondary attribution (gauṇa), metaphorical usage (lākṣaṇika), or by implication (vyāhāra), the Veda employs other meanings — who can object to that?" — it is possible to explain the text as pointing to a meaning other than the direct one, even without relying on the commentary made by the Lord himself. [They continue:] Just as one cannot arbitrarily determine, through the principle of "pariśeṣa" (elimination of alternatives), that the plural number [in some passage] refers to the triad — when there is already a more authoritative statement about the "Sāmpadya" — similarly in this case. Or, even granting that [Śrī Rudra] is directed at the Supreme Lord — this does not establish his being the Lord of all (sarveśvara) or his being of a purely sāttvika (Vaiṣṇava) nature.
