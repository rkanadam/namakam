---
96                    Sri Rudra Bhashyam
---

[Note: Page 98 of the source continues from the eighth commentary of the fourth anuvaka, describing various groups among the attendants of the Lord.]

Those who are addicted to sense pleasures (vishaya-lampata); the "gritsapatayah" are their lords — the rest as before.

[Commentary on Mantras 8 onward — continued:]

Among those [listed]: the chariot-drivers (sarathi) are the kshattrih; those who hold back and control the chariot are the sangrahitarah; and between these chariot-controller types there is a sub-distinction; the "karmarah" are iron-smiths (lohakara); the "punjishtha" are those who collect piles of sand-grains (valuka); "nishadah" means forest-hunters; those who carry loops and nets bound to dogs are the shvan (dog-handlers).

[Continuing the fourth anuvaka commentary:]

The groups listed — from those with chariots (rathibhyah) to dog-lords (shvapatibhyah) — represent the various classes of beings in the manifest world, all of whom are saluted as embodiments of the Lord. The scope of these salutations covers beings from the most militarily powerful (those with chariots and armies) down to the most humble and socially marginal (hunters, dog-keepers, and even dogs themselves). This comprehensive enumeration establishes the Lord's all-pervading presence across every stratum of existence, reinforcing the central teaching that the Supreme Rudra is the inner self (antaratman) of all beings without exception.

The Shruti (Vedic scripture) declares: "Verily this whole world is Rudra — to Rudra who rules this [world] we offer obeisance." The salutation of every class of being, from warriors to craftsmen to hunters, embodies this non-dual understanding: the Lord is not merely the sovereign lord over these beings from a position of externality, but is the very self abiding within each form, whether noble or humble, whether powerful or weak.

Thus the fourth anuvaka, in its comprehensive salutation of all forms of being — from divine armies to earthly craftsmen and hunters — celebrates the totality of the Lord's immanence in creation.
