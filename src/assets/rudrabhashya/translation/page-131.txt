The resting place of cows is the gostha (cow-pen); He who abides there is Goshthya. He who abides in the house (griha) is Grhya. He who abides on beds (talpa) is Talpya. He who abides in the dwelling or mansion (geha, prasada) is Gehya. Kata refers to a place so thickly overgrown with thorns and spines that it becomes difficult to enter; He who abides there is Katya. He who stands in a gahvara — a rough, uneven place such as a mountain cave — is Gahvareshtya. He who abides in deep waters (hrida, agadha-jala) is Hridayyah. Niveshpa means drops of dew or frost-water; He who abides there is Niveshpya. He who abides in fine dust particles (pamsu, paramanu) is Pamsavya. He who abides in visible, dense dust (rajas) is Rajasya. He who abides in dry reeds or grass (shushka) is Shushkya. He who abides in fresh, green grass (harita, shadva) is Haritya. Lopa refers to a hard, rough ground where grass and such things have been cut away; He who abides there is Lopya. Ulccha refers to a type of coarse grass such as balbaja; He who abides there is Ulcchapya. Urvya refers to one who abides on the earth (prithvi) — the form is a long Vedic usage (dirgha chand). Surmya refers to rivers with beautiful waves (shobhana urmayah); He who abides there is Surmya. He who abides among leaves (parna) is Parnya. A heap of dry leaves is parnashah; He who abides there is Parnshadya.

           Namo'paguramanaya chabhighnate ca
           nama attikhdate ca prakhikhdate ca.

Now, with two mantras beginning "Namo'paguramanaya," the aspects of the Lord as the discipliner and regulator of the world are saluted. Among those who are to be disciplined — the most sinful — He stands with weapon raised in a threatening posture: such is the Apaguramana (the one who stands threateningly). He who strikes those sinners is Abhighnan (the striker). In accordance with the degree of their sins, He afflicts some lightly — this is Attikhadan (the light affliction); and He afflicts others severely — this is Prakhikhadan (the severe affliction). Such is the intended meaning. This entire collection of mantras from "Namah somaya" onward, comprising twenty-one separate inner mantras in the form of salutations, constitutes one single mantra. Its Rishi (sage) is the Lord Himself. The meter is Anushtup. The deity is the Lord Himself. The meditation verse (dhyana shloka) from the third anuvaka begins with "rupa, yavvana" etc. This mantra yields as its fruits — equal standing with the sages and lords, and the attainment of the Shiva state. Mastery of this mantra is achieved through five hundred repetitions (japa), preceded by the performance of two kricchra (penances).
