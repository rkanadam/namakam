SRI RUDRA BHASHYAM.                       53

        With an auspicious speech we address You, O Girisha — so
        that thereby all our world — cattle, sons, and the rest — may be
        free from disease and joyful.

Thus, having offered salutation in "namaste rudra," having prayed in the
second, third, and fourth [mantras] for the attainment of the desired and
the removal of the undesired by addressing Girisha — now [the devotee]
prays for the averting of his own fears along with a hymn of praise:
"shivena vacasa" — the word giri signifies the Vedas in their form as
syllables/speech; the Shruti confirms this: "tam ha trin girirupa-
navijnataniva darshayamcakara" (He showed them, as it were, three forms
in the shape of giri), and then explains: "veda va ete" (these are indeed
the Vedas). He who rests in (shete tishthati) the Vedas in their form as
giri (sacred syllables) is Girisha — the one who is declared/described by
the Vedas. O Girisha, we address (vadamasi, we extol) You with pure
(accha, unstained) and auspicious speech (shivena vacasa).

What kind of praise? That is described: yatha — in the manner by which,
when such praise is offered, all (sarvam it, all of it) our world (jagat,
the world of moving things in the form of cattle, sons, etc.) may be
ayakshma (free from disease), sumana (endowed with cheerfulness, happy),
and asata (may be so) — in such a manner do we praise [You]. Here, the
address "girisha" indicates that praise of the one who is declared by all
the Vedas is entirely fitting.

Therein, one might suspect that the words of a sinner are tainted by
association with sin. To counter this, the [deity] is addressed again with
"accha" (O pure one). Nothing adverse results from the association of the
pure (nirvadya) with the impure, even in such contact; just as the purity
of Ganga-water is not diminished by contact with gutter-water — rather,
by proximity to the Ganga, the very gutter-water itself becomes purified
and eventually becomes indistinguishable from pure water. Similarly, words
spoken by me that are directed toward the supremely pure Lord become
as worthy of respect as Vedic words — such is the intent of the address.
Hence, just as gutter-water fallen into the flow of the Ganga acquires the
character of the Ganga, so too my words acquire supreme auspiciousness
(shiva-tva) from such glorious association — with this thought the phrase
"shivena vacasa" is uttered.

This fifth mantra is in fact two verses (the fourth and fifth) combined into
one mantra. The seer of this mantra is of the Gautama lineage; the meter
is Gayatri; the deity is Rudra. The dhyana-shloka is: "May one meditate
upon the god blazing in his three-city-destroying form — the half-smile of
laughter, his divine bow ready to break the pride of the demons, mounted
on a chariot made of earth." Through these two mantras, [protection from]
untimely death and similar benefits are obtained. Perfection (purashcarana-
siddhi) is attained through continuous recitation for twenty-one days.

Thus [ends] the fifth mantra.

        The divine healer (daivya-bhishak), foremost among those who speak
        on behalf of [their devotees], has declared this — destroying all
        serpents and all evil spirits (yatudhanyah).
