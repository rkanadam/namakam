[continuation: the Kūrma Purāṇa passage] — thus establishing those outside the Vedas as great sinners (mahāpātakins), [the text continues to describe how the sage's curse excluded them from Vedic society, and how the Lord, out of compassion, devised alternative paths for their gradual spiritual elevation].

[The commentary continues regarding the third mantra of the Second Anuvāka:]

In this way, the compassionate Lord composed the Tantras as a recourse for those who are incapable of entering the Vedic path, so that they too might have some means to attain merit and gradually progress toward liberation. This is the proper view. Thus "pathīnāṃ pati" (lord of paths) encompasses him as the lord of all paths — Vedic and Tāntrika alike — by virtue of his compassionate omniscience.

The colour "saspiñjara" (golden-red, like the colour of young grass mixed with the redness of the dawn) is an attribute pointing to the Lord's all-pervading creative energy — he who illumines all paths as "tviṣīmat" (the radiant, effulgent one). The connection between his brilliance and his lordship over paths is therefore intrinsic: just as the sun illumines all roads enabling travellers to find their way, the effulgent Lord illumines all spiritual paths enabling seekers to progress toward liberation.

Thus [the third mantra of the Second Anuvāka] salutes the Supreme Lord as: the golden-red one (Saspiñjara), the brilliantly radiant one (Tviṣīmat), and the lord of all spiritual paths both Vedic and non-Vedic (pathīnāṃ pati) — with the implicit understanding that his lordship over all paths is an expression of his boundless compassion and omniscient wisdom by which he guides all beings, wherever they may stand on the spectrum of spiritual development, toward ultimate liberation. Thus [ends the commentary on the third mantra of] the Second Anuvāka.
