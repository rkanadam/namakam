---
82                    Sri Rudra Bhashyam
---

[Mantra 9:]
Obeisance to Rohita (the red-colored one), to the Sthapati (the lord-who-stands), to the Lord of trees — obeisance.

Now the ninth [commentary]: — "Namo rohitaya" (Obeisance to Rohita). "Rohita" means the one of red (lohita) color; "sthapati" means the lord who both stands (sthata) and protects (pata) — [this is] a word of the prshodaradi class (a grammatical class of irregularly formed words). Obeisance to such a one who is the Lord and master of trees — this is the meaning. Here the word "sthapati" conveys the reason for His lordship over trees: it is fitting that He who dwells within trees and protects them should be their lord. The word "rohita" (red) befits the Supreme Lord as the presiding deity of trees, for it will be stated later: "They who in trees are yellow-green, blue-necked, and deep red." Thus ends the ninth.

[Mantra 10:]
Obeisance to the wise counsellor (mantrin), to the merchant (vanija), to the Lord of thickets — obeisance.

Now the tenth [commentary]: — "Mantrine" (to the wise counsellor). The dense growths of forest-shrubs and the like are called "kakshas" (thickets); obeisance to their Lord — this is the primary meaning. But [one might object]: How can the Lord exercise sovereignty over what is exceedingly dense and impenetrable? Addressing this doubt about the impenetrability [of thickets], the text specifies with the word "vanijaya" (to the merchant): A body of merchants is called "vanija"; just as a merchant comprehends and makes clear all the dealings of a country, so too this [Lord] comprehends all things — nothing is impenetrable to Him — such is the intent. Another reason for His lordship over thickets is given
