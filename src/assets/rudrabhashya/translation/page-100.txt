Through these twenty-six mantras ending with "iṣumadbhyaḥ," the Lord's nature as the totality of both sentient and insentient creation has been described — first by demonstrating that each individual entity within the manifest world is a form of the Lord, and then directly and explicitly in the mantra "viśvarūpebhyaḥ" ("to Him of universal form"). Moreover, this same all-soul-hood (sārvātmya) is also implied by the forthcoming mantra "namo jyeṣṭhāya ca" ("homage to the eldest"), which thereby establishes the non-duality of Śiva (śivādvaita). In this spirit, the Śrutis (Vedic scriptures) declare: "That which calms the world-appearance, which is peaceful, auspicious (śiva), and non-dual — that is called the Fourth" [i.e., turīya, the transcendent state]; "There is neither being nor non-being; only Śiva alone exists"; "Rudra is one alone; He does not stand for a second"; "Whatever exists as this universe — all beings, all worlds, manifold and diverse, born and yet to be born — all this is indeed Rudra"; "All indeed is Rudra"; and "I alone was in the beginning, I continue now, and I shall be in the future — no other exists apart from Me." The Smṛtis (remembered texts) also affirm: "The beings are created by Śambhu, Śambhu knows them, the mountains are Śambhu, and Śambhu Himself is all" — and so forth.

Among these, the oneness of the sentient with Pure Consciousness (caitanya) is established through thousands of proofs such as "That art Thou" (tat tvam asi) and "I am Brahman" (aham brahmāsmi). The nature of the insentient, however, is explained through the principle of the Ārambhaṇādhikaraṇa (the topic of superimposition in Brahma Mīmāṃsā, i.e., Vedānta): since the superimposed entity (adhyasta) has no existence apart from its substrate (adhiṣṭhāna) — as is seen in the case of silver superimposed on a nacre shell — just as the shell becomes the substrate of the silver-superimposition and is therefore said to be "of the nature of silver," so too Brahman, as the substrate of the superimposition of the world, is said to be "of the nature of the world."
