Now the eighth [commentary]: — "Ushnishine" (to the turban-wearer). Those who steal (apahrita) land — such as houses and fields — are called "kulunjchas" (land-thieves); obeisance to their Lord. There are two types of kulunjchas: some, who live among the people while wearing turbans (ushnisha) and being [outwardly] reputable, steal fields from wicked people; others, living in mountains and forests as apparently virtuous persons, steal fields and the like from ordinary people. Both of these forms are indicated in order by the two epithets "ushnishine" (turban-wearer) and "girichara" (mountain-roamer). The idea is that it is fitting that the Lord of such kulunjchas should be of such a form.

[Mantra 9:]
Obeisance to the arrow-bearers and the bow-bearers — obeisance to you all.

Thus, through these mantras ending with "namo hiranyabahave" (obeisance to the golden-armed one) — each mantra displaying the lordship of the [Lord] over one particular thing — and through the explicit declaration "jagatam pataye namah" (obeisance to the Lord of worlds), His lordship over all creation — celebrated in the Shruti as "This is the Lord of all, this is the ruler of all worlds" — having been stated, [the text] now begins to describe the Lord's being-the-self-of-everything (sarvatmakatva) in the form of His being the very substance of each thing — beginning with "nama ishumanjya" (obeisance to the arrow-bearers) and so on. Those who carry arrows in their hands and bows for striking the worlds — the "ishumantas" and "dhanvavinah" — all who exist in the world in the form of gods, demons, and humans: obeisance to you, O you who are all these [forms]. Since the aspects (amshas) of the Supreme Lord and those who know Him are the more fierce Rudras in their multitude, and because [the address is] intended as a salutation to them, the plural form is used. And thus among these [Rudras], those who are extremely fierce (ghoratara) are the ones addressed
