---
                    Sri Rudra Bhashyam                    81
---

He brings special happiness to those who have taken refuge in Him; similarly, the Supreme Lord as the kshetrapati (Lord of the field) also protects those who dwell in the field — for there is no other protector besides the Supreme Lord. As the Shruti declares: "By whom the born ones live." It is precisely for this reason that [the text] takes refuge [in Him with the words]: "May you protect us on all sides through that [power], free from disease, and bring us across." This is expressed by the word "avatavya" (that by which one crosses over). The expression that He gives special happiness to those who have taken refuge [in Him], even among those dwelling in the field, is conveyed by the word "Rudra" — as the Shruti says: "Rudra, to the departing being, whispers at the ear the liberating [Taraka] mantra by which that one becomes immortal and attains liberation." Where the word "kshetra" (field) refers to sacred sites such as Avimuktam (Varanasi) and the like, there is a special appropriateness of meaning to be observed.

[Mantra 8:]
Obeisance to the charioteer, to the non-destroyer, to the Lord of forests — obeisance.

Now the eighth [commentary]: — "Namas sutaya" (Obeisance to the charioteer). The charioteer (suta) of the world-machine — [this refers to the Lord as] the driver of all beings. As the Smriti (Vasistha scripture) says: "The Lord dwells in the heart of all beings, O Arjuna, causing all beings to revolve by His maya (divine power of illusion), as if mounted on a machine." "Ahantya" means one who cannot be slain — who is not to be killed; the meaning is: obeisance to the Lord of forests and wildernesses, the sovereign over them. Here the word "suta" (charioteer) reveals the reason for His lordship over forests — that of one who drives the world-machine; and the word "ahantya" (the non-destroyer) declares that even though He drives the world-machine, He Himself is not tainted by its modifications.
