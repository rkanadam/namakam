---
                    Sri Rudra Bhashyam                    97
---

[Continuing the fourth anuvaka — eighth commentary, continued:]

...those endowed with greatness through the divine powers such as anima (the power of becoming infinitesimally small); those without such powers are the "kshullaka" (small ones).

Now the eighth [commentary]: — "Namo rathibhyah" (Obeisance to those with chariots). "Rathinah" means those who possess chariots; "arathah" means those without chariots. The nine mantras from the ninth [mantra] onward, ending with "shvapatibhyah" (dog-lords), are clear in their meanings. Among these: "kshattrih" are the chariot-drivers (sarathi — charioteers); those who hold and restrain the chariot are the "sangrahitarih"; the distinction between these two subtypes of chariot-controllers is noted; "karmarah" are iron-smiths (lohakara — blacksmiths); the "punjishtha" are those who collect sand-piles (valuka — sand-workers); "nishadah" are forest-dwelling hunters; those who carry loops and leashes tied to dogs are the "shvani" (dog-handlers).

[Note on the structure of the fourth anuvaka:]

The Acharya (commentator) here completes the eighth commentary by briefly glossing the nine self-evident mantras that form a continuous litany, listing various social and occupational groups — chariot-warriors, chariot-less warriors, chariot-riders, chariot-lords, chariot-drivers, rein-holders, carpenters, chariot-makers, potters, blacksmiths, weavers, forest-hunters, arrow-makers, bow-makers, hunters, dog-keepers, dogs, and dog-lords — all of whom are saluted as manifestations of the Supreme Lord.

The theological principle underlying this section is the doctrine of sarvatmakatva (the Lord being the self of all). Just as the third anuvaka (chapter section) described the Lord in the form of warriors at various stages of combat — stringing bows, placing arrows, drawing bowstrings, releasing arrows, shooting, and piercing targets — the fourth anuvaka extends this vision to encompass all living beings in every condition of existence: seated or lying down, sleeping or waking, standing or running, assembled or leading assemblies, mounted on horses or leading horse-troops. The vision culminates in the comprehensive salutation of all occupational classes, from the noblest warriors to the humblest dog-keepers, affirming that the one Supreme Rudra dwells equally in all.
