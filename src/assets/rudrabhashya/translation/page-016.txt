---
14                             Sri Rudra Bhashyam.
---

[On the Shataraudriya's supremacy in the Upasana-kanda (meditative section):]

The text demonstrates its supremacy in the upasana-kanda through the statement: "Therefore one becomes established in Avimukta (the eternal abode, i.e., Kashi)." Here, "tasmat" (therefore) refers back to the Shataraudriya study mentioned earlier in "He who studies the Shataraudriya"; thus the meaning is: "Through the study of the Shataraudriya, one becomes established in Avimukta." That is, one has as if performed the upasana of taking refuge in Avimukta; one attains the fruit of the Avimukta upasana. By this, the Shataraudriya's supremacy in the upasana-kanda is proven.

For in the Jabala Upanishad, the upasana of Avimukta is enjoined in the words "Therefore one should resort to Avimukta," and its fruit is stated as "As the life-breaths depart from a being, Rudra imparts to him the Taraka Brahman (the saving knowledge of Brahman), by which he becomes immortal and is liberated." That Avimukta upasana is of two kinds: adhyatmika (internal, pertaining to the individual self) and adhibhautika (external, pertaining to a sacred location). Of these, the meditation on the Supreme Lord at the junction of the eyebrows and the nose-tip (i.e., the ajna cakra, the junction of the brow and the nasal passage) is the adhyatmika form; the vision of the Supreme Lord at Kashi — the sacred field called Avimukta — is the adhibhautika form. Such is the settled position of the school of Shankaracharya.

Now, the fruit of both these forms of Avimukta upasana — the knowledge of the Atman called Taraka Brahmavidya (the saving knowledge of Brahman) — is easily obtained through the mere recitation of the Shri Rudra, without the laborious effort required in the upasana of Avimukta itself. For the adhibhautika Avimukta upasana, the Taraka Brahmavidya is not obtainable without dying in Kashi; hence special effort is needed there. For the adhyatmika form, there is no such dependence on dying in Kashi — as the Shruti declares: "One who reaches that [junction of brows and nose] is as if going to Kurukshetra itself; that itself is the sacred assembly of the gods, the seat of Brahman for all beings — for here, as the life-breaths depart from a being, Rudra imparts to him the Taraka Brahman, by which he becomes immortal and is liberated." Nevertheless, even this adhyatmika upasana requires considerably greater effort compared to japa (recitation). This is precisely why — in the Jabala Upanishad, after the teaching of the Avimukta upasana — for brahmacharins who are unable to perform the effortful upasana requiring single-pointedness of mind and similar disciplines, and who desire liberation through mere recitation, the question arises "By the recitation of what does one attain immortality?" and the answer is "By the Shataraudriya," with the supporting reason "By these verily one becomes immortal."

Thus the Shataraudriya — which bestows the fruit of the adhyatmika Avimukta upasana without effort — is shown to be superior to that upasana; and since it is superior to [even] that [most exalted] upasana, its supremacy over all upasanas is proven. For since all upasanas require single-pointedness of mind as their instrument, the Shri Rudra — which generates the same fruit without such effort — naturally surpasses the entire upasana-kanda.

Now, the reason for specifically mentioning Avimukta — when the text could have simply said "one becomes [as if having done] all upasanas" — is as follows: since in the Jabala Upanishad both Avimukta upasana and the Shri Rudra are taught as means of knowledge for the same purpose, there might arise the erroneous impression — following the rule "equality in specification implies equality in substance" — that both are equal as means of knowledge. To prevent this misunderstanding and to demonstrate that even compared to Avimukta upasana the Shataraudriya is superior, the specific mention of Avimukta is made. This is the import, and all else is properly elegant.
