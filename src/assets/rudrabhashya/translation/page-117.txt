                    Śrī Rudra Bhāṣyam                    115

Rudra is He who drives away (drāvayati) the suffering of saṃsāra (worldly existence). This is indeed heard in the Śvetāśvatara Upaniṣad: "When men shall roll up space as though it were a piece of hide, then shall the end of suffering come without knowing Śiva" — establishing the impossibility of the cessation of suffering without the Supreme Lord, much as one cannot grasp the sky. In order to elaborate upon this, the mantra specifies "namo somāya" — Soma is the one who is united with Amā (Amā-sahitaḥ). The syllable Amā designates Praṇava (the sacred syllable Om) in its aspect of primordial power — this divine name is established in the Laiṅga and other [Āgama] scriptures as "the supreme power is Praṇava" (parāśaktiḥ praṇavaḥ). Through this name, the supreme divine power of consciousness (pārameśvarī cic-chakti) — the presiding deity of the supreme Brahma-vidyā (knowledge of the Absolute) — is denoted.

      Thus, since the removal of ignorance and karma-residue is accomplished only through Brahma-vidyā, the Supreme Lord who is united with the divine power that presides over such Brahma-vidyā is indicated to be the very one who dispels the suffering of saṃsāra. The status of Umā as the presiding deity of Brahma-vidyā is well-known from the Parāśara [Smṛti] and the Vedānta. The Śvetāśvatara Upaniṣad expounds upon this: "The knowers of Brahman enquire: what is the cause? Is it Brahman? Whence are we born?" — having thus raised the question — "Those who pursued meditation and yoga beheld the power of the divine Self (devātma-śakti) hidden by its own qualities" — and so forth. Through this, the great sages and knowers of Brahman came to certainty about the Supreme God as Brahman, as is explained. This is further elaborated in the Deva Purāṇa: "Once some sages, knowers of Brahman, whose minds were seized by doubt, [sought rest]. 'What is the cause? Whence were we born?
