To show the extent of his visibility, the text says: "All living beings — cows, buffaloes and the rest, all creatures — also see him." Thus the Lord, who is of the nature of the Sun (sūryātman), moves downward (avasarpati) for the very purpose of delighting all beings with his vision. As is heard in the Vedic text: "Indeed, all creatures rejoice when [the sun] rises." That form of the Lord which is pure being-consciousness-bliss (saccidānanda), non-dual, exceedingly difficult to comprehend, and which is attained solely through Vedānta, is experienced in the heart only by those who worship him with attributes (saguṇa-rūpa), as [one might worship the Lord dwelling] in Kailāsa — not by all. Therefore, this [solar form] directly perceptible to all completes the spiritual purpose of every person. Such is the magnificent glory [of the Lord], the meaning is. That Lord, of the nature of Āditya, contemplated upon, may He "mṛḍayāti naḥ" — grant us happiness. The Brahmaṇa text states: "By means of these two mantras, the Blessed Lord who is of the nature of Āditya is to be worshipped." Here, for the [mantra] "Asau yas tāmraḥ," the metre is Āstārapaṅkti; for "Asau yo'vasarpati," the metre is Ṣaṭpadā-jagatī. The seer (ṛṣi) for both is Marutvān, and the deity (devatā) is the Blessed Lord in the form of Āditya. [The dhyāna-śloka is:] "One should meditate upon the golden, radiant form dwelling within the solar orb, with a pure smile, a flashing light, with unceasing brilliance, served by thousands of sages." The purascaraṇa (preliminary consecration) is accomplished by continuous recitation for sixteen days, preceded by the kṛcchra (an austere vow). This mantra removes the effects of the evil eye. Thus [ends] the eighth [mantra].
