                        Upoddhāta (Introduction)                             31

[Continuing the discussion of the interpretation of ambiguous Vedic passages]

...in the [various] seasonal cycles, in [the sections on] the anuṣṭhāna (performance) of saṃvatsara (yearly) and samīpa (adjacent) years, in what are called the extraordinary days of the Gavam Ayana (the year-long soma sacrifice) — just as in those contexts, when there is a dispute about interpretation, the Upaniṣad's own commentary takes precedence over the karma-kāṇḍa (ritual section) commentary, as [the principle states:] "the closer/inner authority wins" — so here too, the Upaniṣad's interpretation (given by the Jābāla) prevails. And since the injunction of reciting in an undertone (upāṃśu-svara, whispering voice), which is drawn from the more specifically applicable text [of the Agnicayana context], yields precedence — just as the rule about a different tone at the Prāyaṇīya [ceremony] is taken as having the force of eulogistic praise (arthavāda) rather than as an independent injunction — and since [the Jābāla interpretation] is more specifically applicable (niravakāśa, without competing alternative application), the stronger [interpretive authority], namely the Jābāla Upaniṣad's declaration "These are the names of the Immortal One" — establishes that this Śatarudrīya is indeed directed at the Supreme Lord.

As for the interpretation [of Śrī Rudra as referring to Agni] that is given in the Agnicayana context, where it stands as the interpretation relevant to the fire-altar construction ceremony — that interpretation is to be understood as having a limited purpose: to indicate that Agni, whose identity with the Rudra described in the [Śatarudrīya] mantra is established, is praised [through the Śatarudrīya] as a means of the mantra's application (viniyoga) within that context. Its scope is only that much — it does not establish that the Śatarudrīya is inherently directed at Agni in accordance with the śruti indicators (liṅga) and other hermeneutic principles. For a mantra that is being applied to a particular purpose (viniyoga) — even if its meaning is illuminated through secondary (gauṇa), figurative (lākṣaṇika), or supplementary (abhyāhāra) modes of interpretation — does not on that account have its primary referential meaning (svārthaparatva) replaced by that derivative meaning. This very point is made in the Mīmāṃsā commentary (Vārtika) passage: "Whether by secondary attribution (gauṇa) or by figurative usage (lākṣaṇika)..." Indeed, for this very reason, even though the Aindrā [Ṛc verse] is applied in the Gārhapatya worship, the Aindrā Ṛc is not thereby understood as referring to Gārhapatya [fire]. And for the same reason, even though the Puruṣasūkta is applied in the Puruṣa-medha (human sacrifice) for the praise of the sacrificial persons — as stated in the Śatapatha Brāhmaṇa: "...having stood apart from Dakṣiṇā, Brahmā praises [with the hymn] 'O thousand-headed Puruṣa'" and in the Āpastamba Sūtra: "Having performed [the rite], standing apart toward the south, the Brahmā [priest] praises with the Puruṣa Nārāyaṇa [verse]..." — and even though it is applied for the installation of a human-shaped [fire-altar structure] in the Mahāgni rite, as stated in the Āpastamba Sūtra — and even though it is applied in the worship of Varuṇa, Dhanada (Kubera), and others in the Śātatapa Smṛti and elsewhere — it is not understood as referring to those deities. This is a topic that most scholars address. Therefore, though the blessed Śrī Rudra Upaniṣad is sought by many [to be directed toward Agni or other purposes] and is pulled in various directions through many arguments — like a faithful wife who is faithful in heart even when being dragged away by many deceivers — the heart of the blessed Śrī Rudra Upaniṣad stands firm in its reference to the Brahman who is Śiva and Parameśvara (śāmba-parabrahman).
