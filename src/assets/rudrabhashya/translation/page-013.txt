Introduction (Upoddhatah).                                     11
---

[Continuing on the supremacy in the upasana-kanda:]

"...a being, Rudra imparts to him the Taraka Brahman, by which he becomes immortal and is liberated." Nevertheless, even the adhyatmika upasana requires considerably greater effort compared to mere recitation (japa). This is precisely why, in the Jabala Upanishad — after teaching the upasana of Avimukta, for those brahmacharins who are incapable of that effortful meditative practice requiring single-pointedness of mind and similar disciplines, and who desire liberation through mere recitation — the question "By the recitation of what does one attain immortality?" is followed by the answer "By the Shataraudriya," and the supporting reason is given: "By these verily one becomes immortal." In this way, the Shataraudriya, which bestows the fruit of the adhyatmika Avimukta upasana without effort, is proven to be supreme over that upasana; and since it is supreme over that [most exalted] upasana, it is proven to be supreme over all upasanas. For since all upasanas require single-pointedness of mind as their instrument, the Shri Rudra — which generates the same fruit without effort — thereby attains the status of being supreme over the entire upasana-kanda.

Now, the specific mention of Avimukta — when it would have sufficed to say "one becomes established in all upasanas" — is for the following reason: in the Jabala Upanishad, both Avimukta and the Shri Rudra are taught as means of knowledge and liberation, and [the text] wishes to show that even when compared to Avimukta, the Shataraudriya is superior. The specific mention [of Avimukta] is to prevent the mistaken conclusion (by the rule of exclusion) that both are equal in their capacity to serve as means of knowledge, since both are taught in the same Upanishad for the same purpose. This is the import, and all else is elegant in its own right.
