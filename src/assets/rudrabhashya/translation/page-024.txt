For if that were accepted, those who hold that Śrī Rudra is not the supreme text — by virtue of its being the supreme means of mental purification surpassing Vedānta and renunciation, and by virtue of its capacity to produce the fruit of upāsanā (worship) in Avimukta (the sacred region of liberation) — would not be able to maintain their position even so. Therefore, the following must be stated:

Among the Upaniṣads designated by the name Vedānta, all of which have already been identified as means of liberating knowledge, the Kaivalya Upaniṣad passage beginning "Whoever recites the Śatarudrīya..." is establishing that Śrī Rudra is the supreme among all of them, in response to the question: "Which is the highest?" In this way, the word 'atyāśramī' (supreme renunciant) in the passage which establishes Śrī Rudra must be understood as proclaiming its supremacy within the section on liberating knowledge (jñāna-kāṇḍa). If it did not serve to establish this supremacy, the word 'atyāśramī' itself would be without meaning — the sentence would become mere groundless assertion (aparyavasthita-vākya) without conveying any intended meaning — and that is unacceptable. Therefore, we rightly conclude that the use of that word necessarily conveys the supremacy of Śrī Rudra within the jñāna-kāṇḍa.

Now, an objection is raised: Even granted all this, it does not follow that Śrī Rudra is supreme among all Upaniṣads, because the Atharvaśiras (Upaniṣad) declares: "Whatever brahmin always studies the Atharvaśiras — for him it is as if a hundred thousand recitations of Rudra have been performed" — thus stating that the fruit of a hundred thousand Rudra recitations can be achieved by a single recitation of the Atharvaśiras, from which it follows that the Atharvaśiras is superior to Śrī Rudra. Nor can one deflect this by saying that the Atharvaśiras statement is mere eulogistic praise (prasaṃsā) without literal force — for then the same would apply to "Let him always or even once recite..." regarding Śrī Rudra.
