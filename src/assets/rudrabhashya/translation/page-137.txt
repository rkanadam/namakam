Sri Rudra Bhashyam                    135

---

   Now the third mantra: "Imam rudra ya" etc. To Rudra — to the one who is empowered by austerity (tapas), the "s" being elided by a Vedic sandhi rule; to the one who has matted locks (kapardine, who wears the jata-juta); to the one whose enemies (vira, adversaries) are destroyed (kshiyamana) — to such a Kshayadvira Rudra, we offer this dedication (mitim, i.e., this concentrated intention and prayer), bearing it forward with steadiness and constancy (prabhara mahi, prakarshena nairantaryarupena dharayamah), meaning: we perform this. What is the nature of this dedication? The text answers: "yatha" (in such a way that). May our bipeds (dvipade) — sons, grandsons and the rest — and our quadrupeds (catushpade) — cows, horses and the rest — obtain happiness (sham); may this be so. The verb form "asat" uses the fifth lakara (Vedic optative), with the Vedic augment "ad" added according to the rule "leto 'datau." And not only for those belonging to us — but also that all creatures (vishvam, all beings) dwelling in this village (asmin grame) may be prosperous (pushtam) and free from disease and suffering (anaturam) — for this purpose we bear this dedication. The Puranic statement is to be recalled here: "Wherever the Rudrashtadhyayi is recited — in a village or even in a city — disease, famine, thieves and other afflictions do not arise." Here, the four words — rudra ya, tapase, kapar dine, and kshadavira ya — contain implicit reasoning for the granting of happiness stated in the latter portion of the mantra. By "rudra ya" is conveyed the removal of suffering (duhkha-drava). By "tapase" is conveyed the power of knowledge, from the Shruti: "Whose austerity is made of knowledge." By "kapar dine" is conveyed the ability to accomplish what no other can — from the etymological analysis: kaparda means matted locks (jata-juta) that protect (palayati) the waters of the Ganga; the one who has these is Kapardi — and from this meaning, it is understood that He alone can bear the Ganga, which no other can bear.
