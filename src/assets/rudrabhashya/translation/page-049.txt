The dhyana-shloka (verse for meditation) is: "May one meditate upon the
great Lord — Maheshvara — who is stringing a blazing divine arrow upon the
bow of Shakuneya (derived from the sacred tree), together with the resplendent
goddess, his body shining with magnificent form, united with the Goddess."

The siddhi (perfection) of this mantra is attained by reciting it eleven
thousand times preceded by the performance of the Prajapatya Kriccha (an
austere penance spanning twelve days). Through this mantra, with
pradakshina (circumambulation) and prostration, one obtains the Lord's grace,
the destruction of sins, and Ganapati-status (the position of the leader of
hosts). The various practical applications of this mantra are to be seen in the
Shataraudra Kalpa at the beginning, and are not written here out of fear of
excessive length.

Thus [ends] the first mantra.

        Whatever arrow of Yours, O Rudra, has become most auspicious,
        whatever bow of Yours has become auspicious, and whatever quiver
        of Yours has become auspicious — with that, O Rudra, grant us
        happiness.

Having propitiated the Lord along with all His paraphernalia through the
first mantra, [the devotee] prays for his own happiness through the second:
"ya te ishuh" — "O Rudra, whatever ishuh (arrow) of Yours has become
shiva-tama (most auspicious, most peaceful) — meaning: although it was in
its prior state of a terrible form for the wicked, it has become peaceful in
form through [our] salutation — and whatever dhanuh (bow) of Yours has
become shivam (auspicious in form), and whatever sharavya (quiver) of
Yours has become shiva — sharavya is a particular weapon that serves as an
