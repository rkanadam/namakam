Now the fourth [commentary]: — "Namo'syattva" (Obeisance to those who shoot). "Astana" means the [act of] the released arrow traveling up to the target — those who do this are the "asyantah" (shooters); "vedha" means the penetration of the target by the arrow that has reached it — those who do this are the "vidhayantah" (piercers). The five mantras from "namo shachinebhyah" (obeisance to the auspicious ones) up to "ashvapatibhyanca vo namah" (obeisance to the horse-lords) are clear in their meaning. This third anuvaka (chapter section), which takes the form of a single mantra composed of a collection of sub-mantras in the form of salutations on both sides and having eight parts, is [the subject of the following specifications]: Its rishi (seer) is the Lord Himself; the deity is He Himself; the meter is Brihati (a Vedic metrical form). The meditation verse (dhyana) is:

"Endowed with beauty and youth like a forest goddess; adorned above like a champaka and mango tree in full bloom. Her crown formed like a peacock displaying its twenty-eight constellations; her face resembling the spotless, full autumn moon. Clothed in freshly opened lotus garments and a fine-worked hide; adorned with a garland made of arrows held in both right and left hands. With a quiver hanging down to the navel region, like a kadamba bunch from a tree; with a bow which, though merely to be looked at, is a sight to behold. With beautiful dancing hair adorned with a girl's ornaments on the crown; a mirror-like form, shining and pure like another woman. In whose hand is placed a bow, with a single pure arrow; whose shoulder [the Lord] embraces with His left arm. Smelling again and again a fragrant flower-cluster with His hand; being slowly gazed upon [by Her] with a forest-lotus branch. Surrounded by roe-deer and lovely birds with charming sounds. The world-teacher going and returning, lovely and worthy of contemplation. Such is the Supreme Sovereign of great radiance, in the form of a forest-hunter (Kirata)." This mantra bestows the fruits of protection of one's own kingdom, conquest of enemy kingdoms, and the destruction of [dangers]. This mantra is perfected by reciting it five thousand and one times, preceded by the Prajapati [rite]. After that one becomes fit to employ it [in practice].

Thus ends the third anuvaka.
