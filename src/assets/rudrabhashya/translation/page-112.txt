The primordially existing cause, such as clay, is Pūrvaja (the prior-born); the subsequently arising effect, such as a pot, is Aparaja (the later-born) — the meaning being: "to Him who is the essence of both cause and effect." Madhyama (the middle one) is a youth; Apagalbha is a child whose faculties have not yet matured — the meaning being: "to Him who is of both forms."

      The salutations beginning with "Namo jaghanyāya" establish His inner presence in all things (antaryāmitva). He who is in the rear region (jaghane, the hind part) is Jaghanya; He who is at the root/bottom (budhne) is Budhnya. Sobha is this human realm that abides together with both merit (puṇya) and demerit (pāpa), as the Śruti declares: "Through merit he leads [the soul] to meritorious worlds; through demerit to sinful worlds; and through both he leads to the human world" — He who dwells in that [realm] is Sobhya. Pratisara is the world of moving beings (jaṅgama-prapañca), that which moves and goes (pratigacchati) — He who dwells in that is Pratisarya. He who dwells in the realm of Yama (yama-loka) is Yāmya. He who dwells in the realm of heaven (svarga-loka, called kṣema — the place of well-being) is Kṣemya. Urvarā is the earth known for all grain (sarva-sasyā bhūmiḥ) — He who dwells there is Urvaryā. Khala is the threshing ground for winnowing grain — He who dwells there is Khalya. He who dwells in the Vedic hymns (śrokeṣu veda-mantreṣu) is Śrokya. Avasāna is the end of the Veda, i.e., the Vedānta — He who dwells there is Avasānya. He who dwells in the forest (vane) is Vanya. He who dwells in thickets and shrubs (kakṣeṣu gulmeṣu) is Kakṣya.

      Now the text salutes the Lord by virtue of His being the Soul of all, beginning with "namaḥ śravāya" etc. Śrava is sound, that which is heard (śrūyate iti śravaḥ); Pratiśrava is echo (pratiddhvani); Āśru is a swiftly moving army (āśru — swift; the one whose army is swift) — hence Āśuṣeṇa; similarly Āśuratha. Śūra is the heroic one. Avabhindan is the one who splits asunder — the fire cleaves (bhinatti) the clusters of enemies. Varmin is clad in armour like a tortoise-shell (kacchapa-kaṃcuka).
