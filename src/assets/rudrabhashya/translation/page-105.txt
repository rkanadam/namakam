As the Mahābhārata describes: "Having said 'so be it' to Viṣṇu and made Him the supreme arrow, having fashioned the salt-mountain (śālya) as the arrowhead, the Great Lord crafted the bow of the syllable Om; and the Great God made the Gāyatrī as the bowstring" — indicating the Lord who is equipped with many bows such as the cosmic Meru, Pināka, and others. Through this name, the glory of the destruction of the Triple Cities (tripura-saṃhāra) is indicated, well-attested in the Śruti: "There were three cities of those demons."

      Homage to Giriśa and to Śipiviṣṭa;
      homage to Mīḍhuṣṭama and to Iṣumat.

      Now with two [mantras], the commentator shows the Lord's nature as the Trinity (trimūrti) and His supremacy over the universe. "Namo giriśāya" — the mountain (giri) is Kailāsa; he who possesses it [as his abode] is Giriśa, with the suffix śa in the sense of "possessing" in the class of derivatives known as lomādi (a Pāṇinian class); the meaning is: "to Him who is of that form [i.e., the one who dwells on Kailāsa]." Śipiviṣṭa is Viṣṇu — "Viṣṇu is Śipiviṣṭa," says the Śruti — meaning: "to Him in the form of Viṣṇu." Mīḍhuṣṭama is Hiraṇyagarbha, the creator of the universe, who is supreme in virility (vīrya) and is possessed of a slightly lesser degree of that power; the meaning is: "to Him in the form of Hiraṇyagarbha." Through this, the nature of the Lord as the Trinity — "He is Brahmā, He is Hari, He is Indra, He is the Imperishable Supreme" — as declared by the Śruti, is affirmed.

      He who possesses excellent arrows (iṣavaḥ) is Iṣumat (the arrow-possessor), with the suffix matuP in the sense of distinction (praśaṃsā); and the excellence of the arrow is stated in the Śruti: "That arrow he fashioned, aimed it at the demons, Soma as the shaft, Viṣṇu as the point" — and so forth.
