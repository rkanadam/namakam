[On the Shataraudriya's supremacy in the Jnana-kanda (section on knowledge):]

The text demonstrates the jnana-kanda supremacy through the statement: "One of whatever ashram should always recite the Rudra, or at least once. By this, one attains knowledge — the destroyer of the ocean of samsara."

Here, through the phrase "always, or at least once" (sarvada sakrid va), offering the option that even a single recitation of the Shri Rudra can produce the attainment of knowledge, its argument for jnana-kanda supremacy is established. For it is settled in the "Avritti-adhikarana" (the section on repetition in the Brahmasutras) that the jnana-kanda consists of the Upanishads, and that they are to be studied repeatedly in order to attain knowledge. Thus, since Upanishads in general are to be repeated [for knowledge], and since the Shri Rudra produces knowledge even through a single recitation, its supremacy over the entire jnana-kanda is proven.

Although — just as with other Upanishads — repeated hearing, reflection, and deep meditation (shravana, manana, nididhyasana) until the direct realization of the Brahman-Atman identity is also applicable to the Shri Rudra Upanishad, nevertheless there are two modes by which knowledge arises here: one is through the method of hearing, reflection, and so on; the other is through the method described in the Jabala Shruti — "By these verily one becomes immortal" — whereby, without hearing and the rest, by mere recitation alone, the divine sacred names of the Supreme Lord — names that are themselves eager to liberate — bring about the arising of knowledge through the grace of the Lord, who thereby approaches [the devotee] of his own accord.

In the first mode, the repeated recitation of the Shri Rudra is applicable, prompted by the practice of hearing and so on. In the second mode, through the prior conditions prescribed in the Kurma Purana — namely devotion to sacred ash (bhasmanishtha) and firm absorption in the Supreme Lord — knowledge arises through Shiva's grace from the daily single recitation of the Rudra.

Taking the second of these modes as the basis, the intent of the Shruti is: the supremacy of the Shri Rudra over all other means is established.

The statement "One of whatever ashram" (ayashrami) does not intend a restriction (parisankhya) meaning "only for those not in the householder stage" — for in the Jabala Upanishad, brahmacharins [who belong to the householder stage as well] are the questioners —
