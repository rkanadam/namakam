46                    SRI RUDRA BHASHYAM

— in the eleventh anuvaka, there is a prayer to the group of Rudras who are
portions of the Lord — such is the purport of the eleven-chapter Shri Rudra
adhyaya (recitation).

Now, first of all, in order to propitiate the Lord for the removal of sins
accrued through three [previous] lifetimes, [the devotee] offers salutation:
"namaste rudra" — "Salutation to You, O Rudra."

O Rudra — O You who drive away the suffering of those who prostrate [to
You] — to Your manyave (wrath, anger), namah (salutation) be. One should
not object that wrath cannot be attributed to the Lord who is the ocean of all
auspicious qualities, free of all defects — for wrath directed toward the
wicked is not a blemish when it serves the purpose of restraining the guilty
and favoring the righteous; the Lord Himself has said: "The Shruti and Smriti
are My commandment; whoever transgresses that commandment becomes My
enemy and is to be punished by Me." Nor should one raise the objection that
this implies partiality (vaishamy a) or cruelty (nairghrunyam), since the
Brahma-mimamsa (the Vedanta philosophy, i.e., the Brahmasutras) explains in
detail that the Lord's bestowal of grace and punishment follows the [prior]
karma of individual beings. Here the vocative "rudra" is significant: the word
Rudra is etymologically derived by the Puranic tradition as meaning "one who
causes distress to flow away (dravayita)" — "He who causes suffering (ru) or
the cause of suffering (rut) to flow away (dravayita) — that compassionate one
is called Rudra; Shiva is His supreme cause" — thus the word is analyzed.
Accordingly, since You are the one who drives away the sins that are the cause
of the suffering of the prostrated devotee, it is entirely fitting that wrath
arising from [those] sins should be removed upon prostration.

Thus, the intent of the vocative "rudra" is evident. Having thus propitiated
the Lord, [the devotee] propitiates [the Lord's] weapons too with salutation:
"uto ta" — "uto" means "also, moreover." O Rudra, to Your ishave (arrow),
namah (salutation) be. Similarly, to Your dhanvane (bow) — the source from
which the arrow is released — namah be. And moreover, to the two arms
occupied by the bow and arrow, namah be. The sequence is: the Lord becomes
angry at the sinner who transgresses the divine commandment in the form of
Shruti and Smriti; for the purpose of striking that [sinner], He reaches for His
arrows; fixing His gaze on the bow from which those arrows are drawn, He
extends His arms to grasp the bow and arrow. In this very sequence [the
salutation] is offered here. The divine weapons are, as it were, forms of the
Lord's divine power — a power without limit and infinitely varied — worthy of
worship by the world, just like the divine forms of the deity used in worship.
Hence salutation and propitiation directed toward them are entirely appropriate.

Indeed it is heard in the Mahabharata: when Krishna and Arjuna journeyed to
Kailasa to recover the knowledge of the Pashupata weapon (which had been
forgotten), and having received the Lord's command went to fetch it, the
weapon — the Pashupata astra (the weapon of Pashupati, i.e., Shiva) — was
stationed there, terrible and exceedingly fearsome, with flames shooting from
the mouths of innumerable serpent-hoods. The power of Shri Krishna's
recitation of the Shataraudriya chapter (i.e., Shri Rudra) caused it to
become pacified and come to hand. This episode should be kept in mind here.

Now the seer (rishi), meter (chandas), and deity (devata) of this mantra are
to be indicated, for without knowing these a mantra recitation is prohibited.
Hence it is recited by the Chandogas (followers of the Samaveda): "He who
performs a rite or causes another to perform a rite with a mantra without
knowing [its] authorship (arsheya), meter (chandas), and deity (devata) in the
manner prescribed by the Brahmana text becomes a stump, falls into a pit, or
