                    Śrī Rudra Bhāṣyam                    101

The well-known Śruti passage establishes His role as the bestower of liberation (mokṣahetutva). "He who destroys (śṛṇāti hinaṣti) everything is Śarva"; through this name, the role of Śarva as the destroyer of the universe (jagat-saṃhartṛtva) is stated, as attested in the Śruti: "To that great Time who gathers all in — salutation." By the word Paśupati (lord of souls/beasts), the role of the Lord as the cause of bondage and sustenance is stated: the word paśu (animal, bound soul) refers to the bondage of saṃsāra, and the word pati (lord) refers to protection and enjoyment of that. Accordingly, the Śruti — "He is the cause of saṃsāra-liberation, sustenance, and bondage" — affirms that He is the cause of world-sustenance and bondage; hence, it is concluded that the meaning of these two mantras is homage to the Lord as the cause of the five cosmic functions relating to the herd of souls. Here, to show that the word namaḥ connects individually with each of the four sets of paired names, the conjunctive particles "ca" (and) serve to indicate coordination. Thus the connection runs: "namaḥ bhavāya ca" and "namaḥ śarvāya ca." The same should be understood for all the other pairs throughout.

      Homage to Nīlagrīva and to Śitikaṇṭha.

      Now the third [mantra]: "Namo nīlagrīvāya" — He whose throat and neck (grīvā, kaṇṭha) are blue from drinking the Kālakūṭa poison is Nīlagrīva (the blue-throated one); He whose throat was white (dhavala) before the event of the poison-drinking is Śitikaṇṭha (the white-throated one). By this mantra, the Lord's compassion for all worlds is indicated — His compassion that moved Him, disregarding the natural defect inflicted on His otherwise fair throat by the Kālakūṭa poison, to swallow the venom for the single purpose of protecting the world. This is well-attested in the Śruti: "You have compassion upon this entire world" — and similar passages.

      Homage to Kapardī and to Vyuṣkeśa.
