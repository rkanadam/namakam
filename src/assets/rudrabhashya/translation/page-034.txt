As for the argument that, since Śrī Rudra is characterized by salutations and by prayers for attaining desired things and avoiding undesired things, its true and ultimate meaning — lordship over all (sarveśvaratva), inner-controllership of all (sarvāntaryāmitva), and the like — cannot be established; to this we say: Salutation (namaskāra) is motivated by the excellence of the one being saluted — this is how it is observed in ordinary life as well. Scholars of semantics (śābdakāra) also define namaskāra as an act of reverential gesture preceded by the devotee's contemplation of the greatness of the being to whom reverence is being paid. In inquiring into the excellence of the one being saluted, since it would be improper to reject the excellence offered by the specific epithets in the mantra (śruti-viśeṣaṇa) and adopt some other kind of excellence — the true and ultimate excellence of lordship over all, inner-controllership of all, and so on — as conveyed by the specific epithets 'sat', 'cit', and others in the salutation mantras — is firmly established. What grounds are there for suspecting that it is not ultimate (tāttvika)?

Although salutation out of fear is also observed in the world, and in the śruti itself — "Salutation to your fury (harase), salutation to your light; let your weapons fly elsewhere; do not be harmful to us, be auspicious (śiva) to us" — and one might therefore suggest the alternative explanation (anyathāsiddhi) that this salutation to the Supreme Lord is motivated by his fearfulness (bhīmatva) — still, even a salutation motivated by fear cannot be possible without the one being saluted possessing some excellence. For this very reason, the statement in the Brahman's assembly — "They say, 'Salutation to your radiance (jyotiṣe)'; indeed by making salutation they serve the one who is superior" — explains that namaskāra essentially never abandons the quality of excellence (utkarṣa) belonging to its natural subject (svaviṣaya). In truth, even if it is motivated by fear, it does not diminish the namaskāra [of Śrī Rudra], because the great preponderance of salutations in Śrī Rudra that are peculiar to it (anivara-sādhāraṇa) is not something that would be produced by mere fear.
