[Continuing the discussion of supremacy:]

This entire meaning is demonstrated in the Kurma Purana. There, after describing how Parameshvara (the Supreme Lord) was pleased with the royal sage Vasumanas, who had performed the Gayatri upasana (meditation on the Gayatri mantra) for a hundred years by the power of a boon received from Brahma, the following is recited:

"Then the greatly pleased Mahadeva (Shiva) spoke to the king: 'O sinless one, hear these secret names of mine — sung in all the Vedas, they are the destroyers of samsara (the cycle of rebirth). Worship always with these sacred Name-words, remaining pure. Recite with a single-pointed mind, with your mind fixed on me alone, this Shataraudriya chapter — the supreme essence of the Yajurveda. Let a brahmachari (celibate student), eating sparingly, devoted to sacred ash (bhasma), composed in mind, recite the Rudra [hymn] till death — he attains the highest state.'"

Here, having first established the supremacy of the Shataraudriya within the Yajurveda through the phrase "the supreme essence of the Yajurveda" (yajusham saram), the further use of the word "uttama" (supreme) refers to its supremacy over the Rigveda and other Vedas as well. Thus the injunction is: "One should recite this Rudradhyaya chapter, which is supreme over all the Vedas." By this, the meaning intended by the Jabala Shruti — "Yajnavalkya replied..." — is displayed.

In that context, to answer the question "How is it supreme over all Vedas?", the phrase "destroyers of samsara" is used as a qualifier for the names. The idea conveyed is this: not only does the mere recitation of these divine names destroy samsara, but even prostration (namaskara) to them — since the names are themselves expressions of the Lord's being — accomplishes this. Hence [the text continues]: "Worship always with these sacred Name-words" — since the verbal recitation (vacika) of these mantras that constitute acts of prostration to the Lord is itself a form of namaskara (prostration), one who recites them has also effectively performed acts of prostration. Therefore, if the bare names alone destroy samsara, how much more so if namaskara is also offered to them — the import is that they uproot the suffering of samsara altogether.

By this, the Shruti passage "By these verily one becomes immortal" is explained [through the Kurma Purana]. And the passage "These are verily the names of the Immortal" is explained by "These are my secret names." In this correspondence: "etani" (these) is represented by "imani" (these); "amritasya" (of the Immortal) is illustrated by "me" (mine), pointing to the meaning of the word amrita. "Etani ha vai" (these indeed) corresponds to "sarvavedeshugitani" (sung in all the Vedas), which shows the tradition of occurrence across all Vedic branches — meaning their fame of being present in all the hundred Vedic branches. The word "rahasyani" (secret) indicates that they are not covered by the Naighantuka commentators, since they are secret Vedic terms. The word "anagha" (O sinless one) implies that one who has exhausted his sins through a hundred years of Gayatri upasana has the qualification to engage with the Rudradhyaya without obstruction.

Thus, when liberation is available, yet through the defect of disbelief (ashraddha) the world wallows in samsara — that indeed is the power of sin.
