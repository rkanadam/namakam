[Mantra 4:]
Obeisance to the one who deceives, to the one who thoroughly deceives, to the Lord of sneaky pilferers (stayus) — obeisance.

Now the fourth [commentary]: — "Namo vanchata" (Obeisance to the deceiver). Those who, while being considered as one's own people, having gained the trust of others, steal their goods — they are the stayavah (sneaky pilferers); obeisance to their Lord. In order to establish the Lord's lordship over sneaky pilferers, the two qualifying epithets "vanchate" (deceiver) and "parivanchate" (thorough deceiver) are given: one who, while appearing as a trustworthy person in others' affairs and in transactions of buying and selling of goods belonging to others, steals something here and there — he is called a "vanchashrita" (partial deceiver); one who commits deception in all transactions is called a "parivanchashrita" (complete deceiver); [the Lord appears in] such forms — this is the meaning. Thus ends the fourth.

[Mantra 5:]
Obeisance to the one who roams quietly, to the one who roams on all sides, to the Lord of forest-dwellers (aranyanas) — obeisance.

Now the fifth [commentary]: — "Namo nicerava" (Obeisance to the quiet roamer). Those who live in forests are the aranyavasi (forest-dwellers), and of these aranyanahs, obeisance to their Lord — this is what is being established. [The text] supports this very point with: "Namo nicerave paricara" — one who by nature roams quietly (nicerava) [in forests], surpassing [in stealth] even the forest-dwelling thieves; and "paricara" — one who roams in all directions to steal [more than them]; the idea being that since He is supreme among them by virtue of being a more skilled roamer than even those [forest thieves], it is fitting that He be their lord. Thus ends the fifth.
