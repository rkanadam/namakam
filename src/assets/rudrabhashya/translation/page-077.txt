Śrī Rudra Bhāṣyam                   75

---

Now the third [mantra]: "Namaḥ saspiñjarāya iti" — "saspi" means young grass (ballaraṇa); "piñcara" or "piṭcara" means a mixed colour of yellow-red; hence "saspiñcara" refers to one of that mixed tawny-yellow-red hue; to him. Tviṣīmate — to the one endowed with brilliance (prakāśavate). Pathīnāṃ pataye — to the lord of the Vaidika and Tāntrika paths; to him may there be salutation — such is the literal meaning. Here, by "tviṣīmate" the light of knowledge (jñānarūpa prakāśa) is indicated, and by this a potential objection is dispelled. For an objection might arise here: how could the Supreme Lord have instituted the Tāntrika paths? The Smṛti texts declare: "Those works that are contradictory to the Vedas, and whatever they are — other ill-conceived views — all of these bear no [good] fruit; they culminate in tamas (darkness), and their end is dark." Given that such [Tāntrika works] are censured, how can he be their lord? The response here is as follows: certainly the Tāntrika paths are censured — but that censure applies only to those who follow the Vedic path [and who might turn to Tantra]. For those who are outside the Vedas (vedabāhya), the Tāntrika paths are indeed beneficial. Considering this, the all-knowing Supreme Lord composed the Tantras. If these were not [composed by him], then those who are most sinful, those who have turned away from the Vedic path, who know no other means to auspiciousness, who are prone to self-indulgent conduct and immersed in lowly practices — they would be utterly lost. Therefore, the institution of the Tāntrika paths is appropriate. This has been clearly amplified in the Kūrma Purāṇa, where: "The sage Gautama, having somehow come to know — through the divine illusion (māyā) of that Lord — the cow-slaughter that had arisen among those [people], and angered as if by a curse on a dog, [pronounced]: 'You shall be outside the three Vedas, great sinners —'"
