The Kūrma Purāṇa also states: "Those great-souled sages, knowers of Brahman, having debated many propositions" — after stating this — "[While they were thus engaged,] Gaurī, the daughter of the great mountain, the consort of Mahādeva, appeared before them who were established in meditation and yoga. They beheld through the vision of the Supreme Lord's grace that inner divine being — the cause of all without remainder — the ancient Puruṣa (primal person), the great sovereign, the Lord, the sage, the vast, the ruler."

      In the Kena Upaniṣad too, the allegory of how Indra and the others came to know the Supreme God as Brahman — through Hemavati Umā — is told: "Then in that very space She appeared — Umā of great beauty, the daughter of Himavat. He [Indra] asked Her: 'Who is this great being?' She replied: 'It is Brahman. In that victory of Brahman, you have been glorified.'" Through this account the Kena Upaniṣad establishes that [the gods] came to know the Supreme God as Brahman through Umā.

      In the Kaivalya Upaniṣad: "Meditating upon the Supreme Lord accompanied by Umā — the three-eyed one, the blue-throated one, the tranquil one, the lord of all — the sage attains the source of all being, the witness of all, beyond darkness." This meditation upon the Supreme Lord together with Umā, prescribed as the means of liberation, implies that Umā is the presiding deity of the knowledge of the Supreme Brahman — since Her role as the Lord's liberating consort and the one with whom He is identified as a single reality are equivalent. "Pārvatī, the supreme goddess, bestows Brahma-vidyā — therefore, those who see Śiva together with that power [attain liberation]. For them, the perfection born of knowledge is accomplished; not for others — thus says the Śruti" — and so forth, in accord with all these supporting testimonies.

      Not only does the Lord's association with Umā account for His role as bestower of liberation — but indeed all His greatness: omniscience, omnipotence, inner presence in all, being the cause of all, and so forth — all this is but the play of the supreme power (paraśakti-vilāsa). This the Śrutis proclaim: "His supreme power is heard of as manifold and various — natural, consisting of knowledge, strength, and action" — and so forth. The Itihāsas and Purāṇas elaborate upon this; and the learned also affirm: "Power is the body of this deity; it is the inner Self, knowledge, action, and the instrument — the cluster of desires, the abode, the coverings — all this is You, O crowned one of tender youth [Kumāra, Śiva]. What is there that you are not, O dwelling place of all?" And: "If the deity were not thus [accompanied by Śakti], truly He would not be capable of even a quiver." Therefore, since the great glory of such supreme power of Umā is the very reason the Taittirīya Upaniṣad uses the term "abhīkāpataye" (to the Lord of the beloved one) and then again "amāpataye" (to the husband of Amā, i.e., Umā) — the Lord is thus saluted in His aspect as the Lord of Umā — this much for now.

      Homage to Tāmra and to Aruṇa.

      Now the second mantra — in which the Lord is shown to be the sole object of worship for all, being directly accessible in the form of the sun, the guide and lord of all, and the bestower of all auspiciousness: "Namo tāmrāya" — At the time of sunrise, [the sun] appears copper-coloured (tāmra) within; immediately thereafter, slightly reddish (aruṇa); and from the conjunctive particle "ca," [He takes on] multiple colours further above.
