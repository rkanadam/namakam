58                    Śrī Rudra Bhāṣyam

---

The seer (ṛṣi) of this mantra is Kaṇva, the metre (chandas) is Anuṣṭup, and the deity (devatā) is Śaṃsu. [The dhyāna-śloka for meditation is:] "One should meditate upon the three-eyed Lord (trilochana), who is adorned with a digit of the moon, who bears a serpent as his sacred thread, and who is resplendent like blazing fire." The purascaraṇa (preliminary consecration) is accomplished by reciting [the mantra] forty-five thousand times. Thus [ends] the sixth [mantra].

    "He who is that copper-red one, the ruddy Aruṇa, and also the tawny Babhru, the greatly auspicious one — and the Rudras who dwell in all the directions around this [earth], stationed in the thousands — may we appease their fury (heḍa)."

Thus with six mantras the Lord Supreme has been graciously propitiated. Now, the Blessed Lord who is the witness of all actions and the indwelling self of all beings is propitiated through two [mantras]: "Asau yas tāmra" — meaning: He who was described above, that very Blessed Lord, is Asau, directly visible, manifest in the sun. His visible form is further described by its characteristics: tāmra (copper-red), Aruṇa (ruddy), and Babhru (tawny). At the time of sunrise he is deeply crimson, hence "tāmra" (copper-red); a little later he becomes a pale red, hence "Aruṇa" (ruddy); then he becomes tawny-coloured, hence "Babhru." As the verse goes: "Piṅgala (tawny), Lakula (club-bearer), Khalati (bald), and Viṣṇu — these four are known by the word 'babhru.'" He is "Sumaṅgala" (supremely auspicious) because, by removing darkness and the like, He causes [creation] to awaken and stand; for without the Lord in the form of the sun, the world sunk in darkness would indeed be utterly inauspicious — such is the meaning.
