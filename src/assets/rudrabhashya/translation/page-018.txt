---
16                             Sri Rudra Bhashyam.
---

[Continuing on the jnana-kanda supremacy — the Kaivalya Upanishad passage:]

"...the gods' seat of worship, the abode of Brahman for all beings — here, as the life-breaths depart from a being, Rudra imparts to him the Taraka Brahman, by which he becomes immortal and is liberated." Yet even so, compared to japa (recitation), the adhyatmika Avimukta upasana still requires considerably greater effort. This is precisely why, in the Jabala Upanishad — after the teaching of the Avimukta upasana, for those brahmacharins who are incapable of that effortful upasana requiring single-pointedness of mind and similar practices, and who desire liberation through mere recitation — the question "By the recitation of what does one attain immortality?" is followed by the answer "By the Shataraudriya," with the supporting statement "By these very [names] one becomes immortal."

Thus the Shataraudriya — which bestows the fruit of the adhyatmika Avimukta upasana without effort — is demonstrated to be superior to that upasana; and since it surpasses that [most exalted] upasana, its supremacy over all upasanas is conclusively proven. For since all upasanas in their entirety require single-pointedness of mind as their instrument, the Shri Rudra — which effortlessly generates their fruit — attains the status of being supreme over the entire upasana-kanda.

Now, the reason for specifically mentioning "becomes established in Avimukta" — rather than saying more broadly "has as if performed all upasanas" — is this: in the Jabala Upanishad, both Avimukta and the Shri Rudra are taught for the same purpose as means of knowledge, and there may arise the impression of their equality as means [by the principle that specific mention implies equal standing]. To prevent this [mistaken equivalence] and to show that even compared to Avimukta, the Shataraudriya is superior, the specific mention of Avimukta is made — wishing to show its superiority relative to that comparison. This is the intent of the text, and all else is properly stated.
