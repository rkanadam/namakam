                    Śrī Rudra Bhāṣyam                    105

He who abounds in divine qualities is Varṣīyas (the supremely excellent). Through this name, the glory of the Lord's divine attributes is indicated, as declared in the Śruti: "He who is truth-desiring (satya-kāmaḥ) and whose will is truth (satya-saṅkalpaḥ)" and "His supreme power is heard of as manifold and various — natural, consisting of knowledge, strength, and action" — and similar passages.

      Homage to Vṛddha and to Saṃtṛdhvane.

      Now the tenth [mantra]: "Namo vṛddhāya" — He who is superior to all in age (vayas) is Vṛddha (the ancient, the eldest); He who is venerable to all and for whom no one is an object of veneration — that is the meaning. Indeed, this is heard in the Purāṇa: "He whose head should be bowed to — but who would bow before me? Who would fold his hands in reverence before me? Who could be worshipped by one such as Śikhara? Is not the Lord of all beings [already] venerated by all?" Through this name, the Lord's nature as the object of universal obeisance is affirmed, as well-attested in the Śruti: "To whom all offer triple salutations" (yasmai namas triccharodharme). Though the Śruti declares in general terms "to whom all offer salutations" (yasmai namaḥ triccharaḥ), yet since the statement "You are the most excellent of beings" is made with the Supreme Lord as subject, and the concluding statement is "all their salutations [belong] to Him," the Supreme Lord is indeed the one indicated as the recipient of all salutations in the opening clause "yasmai namaḥ" — this is ascertained. The Lord is described primarily as the support of His own power in the form of the cosmic serpent Śiṃśumāra (Delphinus constellation), and the other deities are described as the head, heart, navel, limbs, feet, and tail of that form — each enumerated separately by their own names. Full detail of this is given elsewhere.
