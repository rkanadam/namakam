In both passages the phrase "the Śatarudrīya is the most excellent (uttama)" raises the question: most excellent in comparison to what? Since the word "Veda" appears in the preceding clause, the most natural interpretation takes the Veda itself — which is already present in the sentence — as the term of comparison (pratiyogi), without needing to posit an unmentioned or inferred counterpart. Thus the supremacy of Śrī Rudra among all Vedic texts is directly and explicitly proclaimed.

The reason for this supremacy is also stated there — namely, that by mere recitation (japa) of it all four human aims (puruṣārtha) are bestowed. As the Lord himself has declared: "He who, in a state of ritual purity, studies this science in the early morning — for such a one who recites the Śatarudrīya in that manner, nothing whatsoever is unattainable." Thus the Lord expresses his own personal conviction that one who recites Śrī Rudra gains all desired objects (sarvārtha-lābha) from his own experience.

By this, it becomes clear — as recorded in the Kūrma, Liṅga, and Harivaṃśa Purāṇas — that the Lord Vāsudeva himself undertook the Pāśupata vow (paśupata-vrata) and recitation of the Śrī Rudra Pañcākṣarī (the five-syllable mantra of Rudra). The Kūrma Purāṇa records: "Here, aforetime, Hṛṣīkeśa, the inner-self of Viṣṇu, son of Devakī, Kṛṣṇa — always observing the Pāśupata vow — dwelt for a year, his entire body smeared with ashes, intent upon the study of the Rudra-adhyāya (the Rudra chapter), worshipping Hari [Śaṃbhu], having undertaken the Pāśupata vow." The Liṅga [Purāṇa] records: "Having thus praised the Great God [Mahādeva] and prostrated before him, the Lord recited Rudra a crore (ten million) times while standing in water." The Harivaṃśa records: "Mukunda [Viṣṇu], his forehead marked with the sacred ash, adorned with ashes and wearing white, wearing matted locks, clad in bark garments, meditating continuously in his mind on the Moon-crested One [Śiva], recited the five syllables [of the Rudra mantra]."
