50                    SRI RUDRA BHASHYAM

— That form (tanuh): of what nature is it? It is answered: It is aghora —
not terrifying by means of weapons without cause; aghora means not ghora
(not terrifying). Not only is it the absence of terror, but further it is shiva —
auspicious, a bestower of happiness. And not merely a giver of worldly
happiness; rather, it bestows the knowledge that is the means to liberation
(moksha), which is the supreme human goal (paramapurusharta) — this is
declared by: "apapakashini." By the word apapa, the knowledge that is opposed
to sin — that is, the knowledge of Brahman (brahman-jnana, the knowledge of
the supreme reality) — is indicated, as per the Shruti: "tad yathaisika-tulam
agnau protam praduyeta evam ha va asya sarve papmanah praduyante" (just as
a reed's tip thrust into fire is burned up, so too all sins of this person are
burned away); and as the Gita states: "the fire of knowledge burns all actions
to ashes." Apapa (freedom from sin) + kashini (illuminer) = one who illumines
freedom from sin, i.e., a giver of knowledge — such is the meaning. [This is
confirmed by] the Shruti: "atmabuddhi-prakasham" (the light of Self-knowledge).

Such being that form of Yours: with that shantamaya (utterly tranquil)
tanva (form), the form that is of the nature of unclouded bliss — abhichakashihi
(shine forth upon us, illuminate us on all sides) — illuminate us completely,
as beings shining on all sides with the non-dual identity of Brahman and the
Self. "May all of this be known when this one thing is known" — [as the
Shruti says.] The form abhichakashihi, being a yak-tug verb form (an intensive),
shows the intensification of the verbal action indicating the seeking of complete
illumination — the meaning being "bring us to a direct, unmediated
realization of Brahman." The phrase "shantamaya abhichakashihi" shows that
the Supreme Lord is of the nature of consciousness-bliss (chidananda).

But, one might think: "If you want liberation through mere salutation alone,
[without] the great efforts of hearing, reflection, and deep meditation
(shravana, manana, nididhyasana), [why use the term girisha?]" — so the
second mode of address is used, correcting this misunderstanding:
