4  Sayanacharya—Bhatta-Bhaskara-Bhashya-Sahita— [Na. Pra. Am.]

Also: "With body smeared with ashes, sleeping on ashes, having conquered the senses, constantly chanting the Rudra—he will attain supreme liberation.
The diseased and the sinful, having chanted the Rudra and conquered their senses, are freed from disease and sin and enjoy incomparable happiness."

Shankha says: "The Shatarudriya is the atonement even for great sins committed in secret."

Apastamba says: "If Rudra should afflict one's progeny or cattle, one should go North and chant the Rudras—this is a ritual procedure not connected with the (main) sacrifice."

In the Ishana-yajna: "Here one chants the Rudras once, middle-wise, or the best way (multiple times)."

And since this is of the nature of the Knowledge of Brahman (Brahma-vidya), this collection of mantras is an Upanishad. Therefore, it is recited in the Rahasya (Secret/Upanishadic) section. We explain it here in accordance with its ritual application in the Brahmanas. This Rudra-Upanishad is recited in all the one hundred and one branches of the Adhvaryu (Yajurveda). It is called "Shatarudriya" because its deity is a hundred Rudras. Through this Rudra-Upanishad, Brahma-vidya is taught.

Brahman has three forms:
One is the effect-form (karya-rupa), which, being the material cause of everything, is the self of all.
The other is for the sake of human goals (purusharthya), as the cause of creation, preservation, and destruction.
Both of these are within the realm of ignorance (avidya). The other, the supreme form (param-rupa), is beyond these, without qualities (nirguna), untainted (niranjana), and characterized by Truth, Knowledge, and Bliss.

This has been said by the seers: "The universe, all beings, the wonderful world, whatever was born in many ways and whatever is being born—all this is indeed Rudra." "Rudra, the great seer, who first from the beginning (perceived) all the gods and their thoughts."

And it is said:
"Victorious is Mahadeva, the Great Lord, who is other than the transmigrating self and the insentient, who is pure, and without beginning or end."
Furthermore: "Presiding over the former group of six, he bears this moving and unmoving world. Presiding over the triad of selves, he performs creation etc."

There, the one who is the Destroyer is always presided over by the God. At the time of action, there is that other fierce (ghora) body of the God. The other is auspicious (shiva). As it is said: "This Agni is indeed Rudra; he has two bodies, one fierce and one auspicious." "In that one offers the Shatarudriya, he pacifies his fierce body by that," and so on.

The power of destruction is always manifold. Those positions of the God as the Destroyer are his fierce bodies; in them, there is fear and doubt. Therefore, by the first anuvaka, one pacifies such an angry God and offers salutations with "Namah" to his means of destruction (weapons etc.) to please him. As it is said: "By that, he pacifies his fierce body. In that he offers the 'Shiva-tanu' (auspicious body) mantra, he pleases his auspicious body." Then, having withdrawn that (fierce form), he offers praise and salutations to the God's universal form (vaishvarupya) through the next eight anuvakas (second to ninth).
