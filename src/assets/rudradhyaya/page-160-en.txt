[Cha. Pra. A.] Rudradhyaya | 149

He prescribes the material for it:

He sacrifices with ghee. Ghee is indeed brilliance (tejas). Vasordhara is brilliance. By brilliance itself he gains brilliance for him. || 2 ||

Sayanacharya Bhashya: The abundance of wealth like gold etc. is also well-known to be of the nature of brilliance. || 2 ||

He praises that oblation as a cause for attaining desires:

Furthermore, Vasordhara is indeed all desires. By desire itself he gains them for him. || 3 ||

He prescribes continuity through the logic of presence and absence (anvaya-vyatireka):

If he (the Adhvaryu) should desire of a sacrificer, "May I cut off his breaths and his food," he should offer it with interruptions (vigraham). Thus he cuts off his breaths and his food. || 4 ||

If he should desire of a sacrificer, "May I connect his breaths and his food," he should offer it continuously (santatam). Thus he connects his breaths and his food. || 5 ||

Sayanacharya Bhashya: If the Adhvaryu desires to cut off the breaths and food of a sacrificer, he should offer this sacrifice with breaks in between. But if he desires continuity of breaths and food, he should offer the oblation continuously. || 5 ||

Now he praises the 144 sentences from "Vajashcha me" to "Annam cha me bhuk-cha me" by dividing them into groups of twelve:

He offers twelve twelve-fold (oblations). Twelve are the months, a year. By the year itself he gains food for him. || 6 ||

Sayanacharya Bhashya: A group of twelve sentences is 'dvadasham'. There are twelve such groups. Thus the number 144 is achieved. Since the number twelve within that group represents the year through the months, he obtains food that is to be gained throughout the year. || 6 ||
