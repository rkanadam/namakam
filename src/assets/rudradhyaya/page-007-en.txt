Because of the hearing of censure for those who do not know the meaning, as stated in verses like:

"He who has studied the Veda but does not know its meaning is indeed a pillar (sthanu) carrying a burden. But he who knows the meaning attains all auspiciousness and reaches heaven, his sins being shaken off by knowledge."

By such sources, one who does not know the meaning is censured as being like a pillar, while one who knows the meaning is said to attain all well-being. Therefore, knowing the meaning is absolutely necessary. "Sthanu" (pillar) is the example. A dry tree stump with its branches cut off is called a "sthanu". Some believe that ritual status is achieved through correct recitation alone, even without knowing the meaning. They argue that the injunction "One's own Veda should be studied" is fulfilled just by grasping the syllables of the Veda. Knowledge of the meaning of the former section (Karma-kanda) is implied by the performance of rituals. Since ritual performance happens through recitation alone, that view is generally accepted there. However, since verses like "One who does not know the meaning and has a weak voice... these are the lowest of reciters" describe the one who doesn't know the meaning as the lowest of reciters, even recitation cannot be perfect without knowledge of the meaning. Although ritual performance is achieved by the mere chanting of mantras, the Chandogas (followers of the Samaveda) declare that a ritual performed with knowledge brings greater results: "Therefore, both perform it—he who knows this thus and he who does not know. But knowledge and ignorance are different. What one performs with knowledge, faith, and the Upanishad (meditation), that alone becomes more potent (viryavattara)." "Study" (Adhyayana) means grasping the syllables preceded by (or along with) the knowledge of their meaning. If that is true, the injunction to study is fulfilled only by grasping the syllables with knowledge of their meaning, not by mere grasping of syllables. The status as a cause of knowledge cannot exist without knowing the meaning. Among modern scholars, there is no disagreement at all regarding the necessity of knowing the meaning for everyone. Thus, it is established that even for its ritual role, grasping the meaning of this Rudradhyaya is necessary.

The means for knowing the meaning include the Nigamas, Nirukta, Vyakarana (Grammar), etc. However, considering that for those who have not put in much effort in those subjects, it is not easy to understand the meaning without a commentary (Bhashya), the minister of King Virabukka, Madhavacharya (also known as Vidyaranya), who was a promoter of the Vedic path, explained all four Vedas. In his explanation of the Yajurveda, this fifth chapter, the Rudradhyaya, was also explained. Thinking that for those who only wish to know the meaning of the Rudradhyaya, acquiring that entire massive work would involve great expense and effort, and considering that its spread should be easy, the head of Anandashrama first began printing this Rudradhyaya along with Sayana's commentary (Sayana-Bhashya). At this very time, a friend named Gangadhar Anant Nitsure, a resident of Wai, sent me a book containing the commentary by Bhatta Bhaskara. Considering that this previously unseen and very excellent commentary by Bhatta Bhaskara should be easily available to scholars, it has also been included here. Thus, this Rudradhyaya, combined with both commentaries, will serve everyone's needs for recitation, teaching, and understanding the meaning, thus fulfilling its role in both ritual and knowledge.
