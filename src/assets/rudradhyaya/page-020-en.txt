[Na. Pra. Am.] Rudradhyaya. 7

Some say: why is the salutation to anger first? Because anger is the root of all afflictions (vyasana). Then, the arrow is saluted immediately after anger, as it is the direct cause of affliction. Then, after the arrow, salutation is directed to the bow, which is its source. Then, salutation is offered to the two arms engaged in both. These—anger, arrow, etc.—are specific manifestations of the God; because they perform actions like anger, they are represented in that form. Thus, by this mantra, the God's fierce body (ghora-tanu), which is engaged in the withdrawal (destruction) of the universe, is pacified.

Now we speak of the preliminary rites (purashcharana) etc. of this mantra: Having performed the Krichhra penance for twelve nights, chanting eleven thousand times continuously for the specified number of days, one completes the Purashcharana. Then, with this (mantra), one should perform circumambulation and salutation. Then, by chanting eleven thousand times near a right-turning (dakshinavriti) [holy place/shrine], all sins are destroyed. Then, by chanting a lakh (100,000) times, sins committed in seven births are destroyed. Having placed fire in that same right-turning shrine, and having cooked ghee and milk-porridge (payasa), one should offer it in one's own fire; he sees the God directly. Having made an altar (kundam) of one cubit in all directions in that same place, if one offers a lakh of oblations with the ghee of a Kapila cow into his fire, he attains lordship over the attendants (Ganapatyam). Now, this verse is told for kings: A king should have the worship of the Linga performed by a Brahmin who has achieved the Purashcharana. That method is told—having made a fire-altar to the south of the Linga, having churned and placed the fire, he should offer eight thousand oblations each of ghee, with a ladle, pure rice, and Sami-wood sticks, as in daily rites. He should chant eight thousand times in the presence of the God. Eight thousand circumambulations around the temple. A 'Tandava' (dance) in the presence of the God. Prostrations and salutations, also eight thousand. By these acts, the Lord becomes very well pleased.

Meditation (Dhyanam):
"One should meditate on Maheshwara, of adorable appearance, accompanied by the Goddess, in the form of a young warrior, placing a shining divine arrow on a bow drawn back to his ear." (1)

|| He speaks the second verse ||

That arrow of yours which is most auspicious, and your bow which has become auspicious;
And that quiver of yours which is auspicious—with that, O Rudra, make us happy. (2)

[Word-for-word]: Ya | te | ishuh | shiva-tama | shivam | babhuva | te | dhanuh | shiva | sharavya | ya | tava | taya | nah | rudra | mridaya || 2 ||

Sayana's Commentary: O Rudra, that arrow of yours which has become most auspicious (shivatama), most peaceful. Similarly, that bow of yours which has become auspicious (shivam), peaceful. And that quiver (sharavya) of yours which is auspicious—with that peaceful arrow, that peaceful bow, and that peaceful quiver, make us (nah) happy (mridaya), give us joy. Their 'peacefulness' consists in the absence of their application against devotees. (2)
