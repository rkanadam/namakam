6  Sayanacharya—Bhatta-Bhaskara-Bhashya-Sahita— [Na. Pra. Am.]

Bhatta-Bhaskara-Bhashya: For 'Ya te ishuh shivatama', the Rishi is Atreya. The Deity is Shambhu. The Metre is Anushtup. O Rudra, that arrow of yours which is 'shivatama'—extremely conducive to the happiness of devotees. It has not merely become an ornament for your hand—this 'has become' (babhuva) is used in the sense of the present tense, as in 'Vedic grammar allows tense shifts'. And your bow which has become auspicious (shivam). Here also, the word 'which' (yat) should be understood. That bow of yours which has become auspicious is conducive to the happiness of your devotees, not just an ornament for your other hand. And that quiver (sharavya) of yours which is auspicious and conducive to the happiness of devotees. The word 'sharu' refers to a weapon, derived from a root meaning injury. It is heard in the Veda: "The roaring one, endowed with powerful deeds, providing shelter, holding the residue of the sacrifice (rijishi)." From that, adding a suffix, the word 'sharavya' refers to things that are thrown, like a discus (chakra) etc. It is heard: "Fly away, O sharavya, with the friendship of Brahman." Because it is a name, it is feminine. Or, it could be formed with a suffix meaning 'beneficial for arrows (shara)', i.e., a quiver (ishudhi). It is feminine as before. Since all the arrows are auspicious, the auspiciousness of their container (the quiver) is implied. Just as one says "May the village be pleased" (meaning the people in it). Or, he prays that it may not be the source of arrows (against us). As will be said later: "Then, that quiver which is yours, place it far away." With that (taya), make us (nah) happy (mridaya). With which? With the quiver mentioned immediately before; and by implication, with the arrow and the bow mentioned earlier. Or, the gender and number are not intended to be specific. With those arrows etc., make us happy—this is the meaning. He prays: "Having taken such a peaceful body, make us happy," for the sake of withdrawing the fierce body.

Having chanted this mantra for twelve days while eating only 'charu' (sacrificial porridge), one becomes fit to use it for specific purposes. If one desires something, he should then offer eleven thousand oblations of a paste of sesame, rice, and gooseberry (amalaka); the desire will be fulfilled. By this, for the removal of famine, a king should have a lakh (100,000) of oblations of charu mixed with sesame and jaggery offered in his fire. Then prosperity arises in the kingdom.

Meditation (Dhyanam):
"One should meditate on the God with a beautiful smile, seated on a chariot with the Goddess, shining with brilliance, with his hands adorned with arrows and bow, having the form of a hero, being praised by the best of gods." (2)

|| He speaks the third verse ||

O Rudra, that body of yours which is auspicious, non-terrible, and revealing no sin—
With that most peaceful body, O dweller on the mountain who grants peace, look upon us. (3)
