76 Sayanacharya—along with Bhatta Bhaskara Bhashya— [Na. Na. A.]

|| Thus ends the Eighth Anuvaka ||

Sayanacharya Bhashya: In the eighth Anuvaka, the Yajus with salutations on one side were mentioned. Now, in the ninth Anuvaka, some others are being mentioned. He states the nineteen Yajus present there:

Salutations to the one in the barren land (Irinyaya) and to the one in the path (Prapathyaya). || 1 ||

Sayanacharya Bhashya: 'Irinam' is barren/salty land; He who is in that is 'Irinyah'. 'Prapathah' is a path frequented by many; He who is in that is 'Prapathyah'. || 1 ||

Bhatta Bhaskara Bhashya: Salutations to 'Irinyaya'. 'Irinam' is barren land; He who is in that. To 'Prapathyaya'. 'Prapathah' is a great path frequented by many; He who is in that. || 1 ||

Salutations to the one in the small pebbles (Kinshilaya) and to the one in the habitable place (Kshayanaya). || 2 ||

Sayanacharya Bhashya: 'Kinshilah' is a gravelly region where there are small, vile stones. 'Kshayanah' is a place suitable for residence. || 2 ||

Bhatta Bhaskara Bhashya: Salutations to 'Kinshilaya'. Where there are small, vile stones, that gravelly region is 'kinshilah'; He who stands there. (By the rule of location, the word denotes the dweller). Or 'kinshilah' is a specific region where there is doubt whether they are small stones or crystals; He who stands there. To 'Kshayanaya'. From the root 'kshi' (to dwell). The suffix 'lyut' is used in the sense of location. 'Kshayanah' is a place suitable for residence; He who stands there. The usage is as before. Others say: All these from 'Shashpya' onwards are specific types of transmigrating beings. 'Shashpah' are those like young grass with very little strength. 'Phenah' are those like foam with very short lives. 'Sikatah' are like sand, extremely small. 'Pravahah' are those with great power and opulence. 'Irinah' are like barren land, with zero power. 'Prapathah' are those on the main path. 'Kinshilah' are those of vile character (kutsita-shila). 'Kshayanah' are those subject to decay. He exists in all of them without distinction as their benefactor. The sense is that He is the grace-giver of all. || 2 ||

Salutations to the one with matted hair (Kapardine) and to the one who stands before (Pulastaye). || 3 ||
