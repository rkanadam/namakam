
[Na. Pra. Am.] Rudradhyaya. 9

...kashini. With that most peaceful body (tanuva shantamaya), O Girishanta, look upon us (abhichakashihi). (3)

Sayana's Commentary: Indeed, Rudra has two bodies. As is heard later: "This Agni is indeed Rudra; he has these two bodies—one fierce and one auspicious." The injurious one is fierce; the graceful one is auspicious. O Rudra, that body of yours which is auspicious and performs grace upon us, is therefore non-terrible (aghora), not injurious. Its non-terribleness is clarified: 'Apapakahshini'—sin (papa) is of the form of injury or undesirable things; 'papakashini' would mean one who reveals such things; he is not like that, hence 'apapakahshini'. He who, staying on the mountain (giri) Kailasa, constantly extends (tanoti) happiness (sham) to living beings is 'Girishanta'. In that vocative case: O Girishanta, with that body which is 'shantamaya'—extremely conducive to happiness—look upon me (abhichakashihi), shed light toward me. The meaning is: having revealed such a body of yours, make me happy. (3)

Bhatta-Bhaskara-Bhashya: For 'Ya te rudra shiva tanuraghora', the Rishi is Kashyapa. The Deity is Shambhu. The Metre is Svarad-Anushtup. Some say Brihati, because it has more than three letters (or based on a specific count). The peaceful body is of two types: with weapons and without weapons. There, the first was described by the preceding mantra. The other body is described by this one. O Rudra, that body (tanuh) of yours which is auspicious (shiva), peaceful, and a cause of fearlessness. 'Tanuh' is a synonym for body, ending in a long 'u'. 'Aghora'—not cruel. To express the absence of the cause of two types of fear—one's own and that dependent on others—even after saying 'shiva' (auspicious), he says 'aghora' (non-terrible) again. He says the same: 'Apapakashini'—one who does not reveal sin or affliction (vyasana), because of the absence of weapons which are the causes of affliction. In both places, the un-augmented form is due to the prefix being an indeclinable. Or, it means one who does not reveal the sorrows which are the fruits of sin, because it is the cause of the destruction of sins for those who remember it. With that body (tanuva), with that most peaceful (shantamaya) form which is extremely conducive to happiness for those who remember it and which is of the nature of unsurpassed bliss. 'Nah'—upon us, 'abhichakashihi'—manifest again and again. The root 'kash' has a Vedic shortening. Or, 'achakashi' means 'looking'. It is a Vedic frequentative. This is the meaning: being such a one with a most peaceful body, look at us with a gaze of grace. O Girishanta—he who, having constantly manifested on the mountain Kailasa, extends happiness to beings. The suffix 'da' is added to the root 'tan' (to extend). Or, the mountain is the clouds; staying there, he extends happiness to beings in the form of rain (Parjanya). Or, staying in the mountain of speech, i.e., in the Veda, he extends happiness in the form of the meaning.
