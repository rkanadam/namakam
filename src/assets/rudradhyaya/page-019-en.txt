Sayanacharya—Bhatta-Bhaskara-Bhashya-Sahita— [Na. Pra. Am.]

...the meaning of the word 'Namah' is 'other-name' (or 'yielding'). O Rudra, salutation be to your anger (manyu), which has taken the form of wrath. The word 'be' (astu) is brought in from the mantra to be spoken later. Rudra is he who makes everyone weep (rodayati) at the time of death. The 'rak' suffix is added. Puranic writers and others say many things: some say Rudra is he who runs (dravati) or makes others melt (draavayati) toward the sound (rut). Others say Rudra is he who looks at or grants Dharma etc. through the Veda (rut). Some say he who grants the object of speech through speech (rut). Others say he who grants self-realization through the sound of Pranava (rut). Others say Rudra is the Great God who, roaring (roruyamanah), enters into mortals. For instance: "The bull, bound in three ways, roars loudly; the Great God has entered into mortals." Others say: Rudra is 'Ruk' (light/radiance), based on the letters 'ru', 'ka', 'e', and 'ra'. Or, Rudra means the radiant one. Or, he who possesses the power that obstructs, binds, or deludes, and who melts it away for his devotees is Rudra. Some say Rudra is he who melts away the sorrow of the world (rudam).

As it is said: "Rudra is the melter of impurity, the taker of the sacrifice, the one born again. Therefore he is Shiva, and here he is called by the word Rudra."

Others say Rudra is Prana (life-force), who gives (raati) the sound (ruti). Still others say Rudra is he who gives the Self of the Veda to Brahma at the beginning of the Kalpa. As it is said: "He who indeed sends the Vedas to him..." Thus, the authors of various systems derive the word 'Rudra' according to their own principles. 'Manyu' comes from the root 'man' (to think/know); it means anger. May your power which consists of anger become of the form of peace. "Do not harm the devotees"—thus he prays for its withdrawal regarding devotees. The same should be understood for the arrow etc. 'Uto' is a combination of 'uta' and 'u', meaning 'also'. Salutation to your arrow (ishu). The Vedic form of the God's arrow is described as: "Agni was its point (anika), Soma was its shaft (shalya), Vishnu was its tip (tejana/feathers)." In another branch: "Of that (arrow), Agni was the point, Soma the shaft, Vishnu the tip, and Varuna the feathers." Furthermore, salutation be to your bow (dhanvan). Puranic writers say: "The Golden Mountain (Meru) was the God's bow."

In the Mahabharata, it is said: "That Maheshwara, having made Omkara his bow, Savitri his string, the four Vedas his horses, and the chariot consisting of all the gods..."

The word 'dhanvan' is a synonym for 'dhanus' (bow). And also (uta-api) salutation be to your two arms (bahu). The arms are well-known. The dual number is used here. Indeed, the God has many arms; this refers to the two arms engaged in holding the devotee's arrow and bow. Here...
