"Whose mother is Shrimati, and whose father is of good fame, Mayana; Sayana and Somanatha are his brothers, like mind and intellect. (1)
Whose ritual manual is the Baudhayana Sutra, whose branch is the Yajushi (Yajurveda), and whose lineage (Gotra) is Bharadwaja—that all-knowing one is indeed Madhava." (2)

As he himself stated in the beginning of his commentary on the Parashara Smriti, it is known that his mother was Shrimati, father was Mayana, and Sayana and Somanatha were his two brothers; his sutra was Baudhayana, branch was Yajushi, and Gotra was Bharadwaja. In some places, the reading "Sayana and Bhoganatha" is also seen. Therefore, it cannot be determined with certainty whether the two brothers were named Sayana and Somanatha or Sayana and Bhoganatha. Similarly, it is not known whether the "Vedartha-prakasha" (the commentary on the Vedas) was authored by Madhavacharya or Sayanacharya. Some modern scholars say that Madhavacharya was entirely absorbed in state affairs, and his brother Sayanacharya, having composed great works like the Vedartha-prakasha, achieved fame under Madhavacharya's name. Just as Hemadri, the minister of the King of Devagiri, is famous as an author through many works actually composed by his protégé, the great scholar Vopadeva, but published in his own name, so also they say this Madhavacharya became famous through works composed by Sayana. In reality, we find no evidence on which this inference could enter the category of proof. In the various works famous as being authored by Madhavacharya, we find titles like "Sayana-Madhaviya" in some places, "Composed by the Minister Madhava" in others, and "In the Madhaviya composed by Sayanacharya" in yet others. Therefore, there is a great doubt as to whether the author of works like the Vedartha-prakasha is Sayana or Madhava. Works composed by Somanatha are not found anywhere. There was a great scholar named Bhatta Someshwara; many Mimamsa works composed by him are found. Whether he is the same Somanatha or someone else cannot be determined. So, let it be; enough of this deep immersion.

Some say the time of Madhavacharya's birth was around the Shaka year 1300. From an available copper plate inscription, it is known that in the Shaka year 1313, in the year named Prajapati, on the day of the solar eclipse in the dark fortnight of Vaishakha, the illustrious Madhavacharya, a promoter of the Vedic path, having renamed the village named Kunjara as Madhavapuri after himself, gave it to twenty-four learned Brahmins with a libation of water. If we take this gift as being given in the year 1313, it is inferred that his birth was in the fourth quarter of the thirteenth century (Shaka).

It is known that in his early life, Madhavacharya first served as a minister for King Virabukka, and later for some time for his son, the one of auspicious name, Harihara.
