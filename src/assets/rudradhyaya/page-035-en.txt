22  Sayanacharya—Bhatta-Bhaskara-Bhashya-Sahita— [Na. Ma. Am.]

...let them be. May the 'nishangadhi'—the container of arrows—of this Rudra be 'abhu', scarcely capable. May it be capable only of holding the arrows by being hung on a peg, but not capable of having the arrows drawn out. Or, 'nishangadhi' means the sword-scabbard; may it also, as before, be capable only of holding the sword by being placed somewhere, but not capable of having the sword drawn out. (12)

Bhatta-Bhaskara-Bhashya: Now the third. 'Vijyam'. 'Kaparda' is the matted hair; the bow of the God who possesses that, named Pinaka, is 'vijyam'—let it be without a string (this is the understood remainder). Now, let the 'banavan'—the quiver in which the arrows are kept—be 'vishalya', without arrows. The idea is: may that which was formerly 'banavan' (possessing arrows) now be 'vijvayah' (without arrows). 'Uta' is used in the sense of 'also'. And also, may the arrows belonging to this God be 'aneshan'—may they not strike. The past tense is used in the sense of a prayer in the Veda. Or: may his arrows remain hidden elsewhere than in the quiver. By that, the preceding statement 'vijvayah' is supported. Furthermore, let the 'nishangadhi' of this God be 'abhu'. 'Nishanga' is the sword, which is bound or kept with a scabbard (kosha); that in which it is placed is the 'nishangadhi'—the scabbard. The 'dh' of 'dhi' is changed to 'th' in the Vedic style, for "the gods love the cryptic." 'A-bhu' means 'diminished' or 'empty'. 'A' is in the sense of 'little'. The scabbard's nature is to hold the sword; its being without the sword is its 'a-bhavanam' (non-being/emptiness). The idea is: let it also be without the sword. Or: 'nishangathi' means the sword itself. May it be 'abhu', of diminished power, its influence blunted.

For these three verses (10, 11, 12), the Purashcharana is: having performed Krichhra, purified oneself, and chanting for nine days, one achieves siddhi. With these, during war, after consecrating the king's weapons, one should offer a thousand oblations of Kusha grass. The enemies brought to the battle will be defeated.

Meditation (Dhyanam) [for Mantra 11]:
"One should meditate on Rudra, who is like crores of rising suns, with eyes full of blazing fire, adorned with terrifying serpents, and holding various weapons."
Meditation for the second [Mantra 12]:
"One should meditate on Rudra, the controller of the gods, holding the trident (shula) and the skull-staff (khatvanga), surrounded by a garland of flames, the destroyer of the pride of the enemies." (12)
