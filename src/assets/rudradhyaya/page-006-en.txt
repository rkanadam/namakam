Om, Salutations to the Supreme Soul.

According to the saying "They praise with Rik verses, sacrifice with Yajus, and sing with Saman," this fifth chapter of the Yajurveda, which is dedicated to sacrifice, is called the 'Rudradhyaya'. In the fourth chapter, all the brick-pilings (for the altar) were described. In this fifth chapter, named Rudradhyaya, the oblation (homa) in the piled fire is described. Since it is recited in the Karma-kanda (ritual section), it is a part of the ritual, as the Shruti states:

"This Agni is indeed Rudra; he is born when he is fully piled up. Just as a newborn calf desires the breast, so he (Agni-Rudra) at this time desires his share. If one does not offer an oblation to him, he might harm the Adhvaryu and the sacrificer. One offers the Shatarudriya; by that share, one pacifies him. The Adhvaryu and the sacrificer do not suffer harm."

The Smriti also says:
"He who daily employs the triad—Chamakam, Namakam, and the Purusha Suktam—is glorified in the world of Brahma."

Yajnavalkya also states:
"One who drinks wine or steals gold, if he stands in water and chants the Rudra (Namakam) and the 'Sahashra-shirsha' (Purusha Suktam), he is freed from all sins."

Thus, its status as a part of the ritual is well-known. In the Jabala Upanishad, its role as a cause of knowledge is also mentioned. When asked by students, "By chanting what does one attain immortality?", Yajnavalkya replied, "By the Shatarudriya." "These are indeed the names of Immortality; by these one becomes immortal." In the Kaivalya Upanishad, it is said: "He who studies the Shatarudriya becomes purified by fire," and so on.

Smritis also state:
"One who constantly chants the Rudra will attain supreme liberation," and many others.

Thus, because of its role in both ritual and knowledge, this Rudradhyaya must be revered by both those seeking worldly results and those seeking liberation. Its recitation and teaching are prevalent everywhere, especially among the people of the South. Although it yields desired results just through recitation, as the Veda says:

"One, though seeing, has not seen Speech; another, though hearing, does not hear her. To another, she reveals her beauty, like a well-dressed, loving wife to her husband."

Because of the praise for understanding the meaning of the Vedas in such verses:

"What is learned but not understood, and is merely repeated by rote, never blazes up, like dry wood in a place without fire."
