159 Sayanacharya—along with Bhatta Bhaskara Bhashya— [De. A. A.]

[Mimamsa (Philosophical Inquiry)]:
Discussed in the Third Pada of the Twelfth Chapter:
"Is the Vasordhara at the beginning of the mantra or at the end? Because of the continuity (santati)...
The first view is: in Homas, it is at the beginning; but here, because of the groups of twelve, the oblation is at the end."

In the piling of the fire, a certain ritual is prescribed: "He offers continuous streams of wealth (Vasordhara)." There, continuity is desired. And that is possible only if the mantra and the ritual start together. Therefore, there is a doubt that the ritual coincides with the beginning of the mantra. But in this Vasordhara ritual, it is heard: "He offers twelve twelve-fold." One oblation is to be accomplished by twelve mantra-sentences. For example:

"Vajashcha me, prasavashcha me, prayatishcha me, prasitishcha me, dhitishcha me, kratushcha me, svarashcha me, shlokashcha me, shravashcha me, shrutishcha me, jyotishcha me, suvashcha me." || 22 ||

Sayanacharya Bhashya: One oblation should be offered with these twelve mantra-sentences. Such are the subsequent oblations. They are to be accomplished by "Pranashcha me, apanashcha me..." and so on. There are twelve such Homas. The 'continuity' (santati) refers to the absence of delay between these oblations. It does not mean the starting together of the mantra and the ritual. Therefore, by the previous logic, there is no obstacle to the ritual coinciding with the end of the mantra. || 22 ||

Thus ends the Eleventh Anuvaka in the Chamaka Bhashya of the Yajur-Samhita, Fourth Kanda, Seventh Prapathaka, in the Madhaviya Vedartha Prakasha composed by Sayanacharya. || 11 ||

|| Thus ends the Bhashya on the Chamakam ||
