In Jain cosmology, space and time were considered limitless similar to the Veda Upanishat's view. anuyōga dvara sūtrā explains about the Infinite numbers, law of indices and uses it to develop the notion of logarithms. Terms like ardhāced, trigāced, caturāced are used to denote log base 2, log base 3 and log base 4, respectively. Jain mathematicians recognized five different types of infinities: 1. infinite in one direction, 2. in two directions, 3. in area, 4. infinite everywhere and 5. perpetually infinite. Permutations and combinations are explained in the bhagavati sūtrā and in Sathananga sūtrā.

Buddhist mathematics was classified either as gaṇanā (Simple Mathematics) or saṅkhyāna (Higher Mathematics) and classifying the numbers as: saṅkhyeyā (countable), asaṅkhyeyā (uncountable) and anantā (infinite).

Philosophical formulations concerning śūnyā - i.e., emptiness facilitated by introducing the concept of zero. While the zero (bindu) in the place-value numeral system appears much earlier, algebraic definitions of the zero and its relationship to mathematical functions appear in the mathematical treatises of brahmaguptā during 7th Century.
