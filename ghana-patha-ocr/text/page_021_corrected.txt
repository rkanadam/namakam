The Other names of this Veda are: 1. brahma Veda 2. aṅgiro 3. bṛṣvāṅgirasā 4. kṣatra Veda and 5. bhaiṣajīya. This had nine branches or recensions (navadha Atharvano Veda): These nine branches are: (1) paipilāda (2) tauḍa (3) muṇda (4) śaunaka (5) jājala (6) jalāda (7) brahmaveda (8) devadarśa and (9) chāraṇa vaidya. Today, only two of the nine branches of Atharva Veda are available to us. They are: 1. paipilāda śākhā (This śākhā was thought to be completely extinct and was known only through one imperfect manuscript from Kashmir. During 1960s, some scholars were discovered in Orissa, and the paipilāda Samhitā was edited and published by Shri. D. Bhattacharya. Few Atharvavedin affiliated to this school exist in South India) 2. śaunaka śākhā (a small group of scholars in Saurashtra (Gujarat), Gokarna, (Karnataka), Varanasi, (U.P) and Tamil Nadu). muṇḍaka and māṇḍūkya are its Upanishads.

"gopatha Brāhmaṇā" is the Brāhmaṇam for this Veda. "kauśika gṛhya sūtra" is used for its application (prayoga) for this Veda. The Samhitā of this Veda has 20 kāṇḍā (chapters), 730 sūktās and 5977 mantras. Some prefer to catalog the Atharva Veda Text (Samhitā) into prapāṭhakas (Lessons or lectures). Under this classification, there are 34 prapāṭhakas that house the 5977 verses. In yajñā the priest is called "brahmā ", (knows mantras of all Vedas) and has a very important place amongst all ṛitviks. Major portion of Atharva Veda is common to ṛig Veda.
